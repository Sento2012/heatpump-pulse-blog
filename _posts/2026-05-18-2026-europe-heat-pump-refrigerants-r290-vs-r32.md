## R290 is growing, but R32 still sets the market’s pace

The European heat-pump catalogue remains far more concentrated than the refrigerant debate sometimes suggests. In a live index of **60,989 listed models from 777 manufacturers**, **R32 is still the dominant working fluid**, while **R290 has moved from niche status into a measurable premium segment**. The numbers are still lopsided: **R32 accounts for 13,935 listings (22.85%)**, versus **537 R290 models (0.88%)**. Even so, propane is no longer an edge case.

That shift matters because the regulatory trajectory is moving in the opposite direction from legacy HFCs. In the corpus used for the analysis, **R290 carries GWP 0**, while **R32 is listed at GWP 771**, **R410A at 1924**, and **R407C at 1624**. The same schedule records phase-out milestones of **2025 for R410A and R407C**, **2026 for R134a**, and **2027 for R32**. In other words, the biggest current volume pool is also the one facing the most obvious medium-term pressure.

The market split is even clearer when you narrow to refrigerant-coded declarations: **R32 holds 84.74% of the coded subset**, **R410A 11.53%**, and **R290 3.27%**. That 3.27% lines up with the overall natural-refrigerant share, showing that propane is doing nearly all of the work inside the low-GWP bucket. What the registry does **not** support is a clean refrigerant-by-refrigerant price or SCOP league table. The data does show that efficiency is more strongly structured by product type than by refrigerant alone: **water-water units average 6.15 SCOP**, **ground-water 4.77**, and the mass-market **air-water segment 4.54**. That suggests refrigerant choice is only one part of the economics; segment, capacity, and market positioning do most of the heavy lifting.
