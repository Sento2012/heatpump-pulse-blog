The Baltic story is less about a clean national brand ranking and more about the limits of what EPREL can reveal at country level. For Lithuania, Latvia, and Estonia, the supplied registry snapshot does **not** expose local model counts, top-3 shares, or refrigerant splits — so any apparent “winner” would be inference, not measurement. What it *does* show is that all three markets sit in a similar cold-climate band, with annual heating degree days clustered tightly around 4,400–4,474: Lithuania at 4,423.05, Latvia at 4,407.08, and Estonia at 4,474.47.

That climate similarity matters because the operating backdrop is still meaningfully different. Electricity prices in the dataset range from €0.1955/kWh in Lithuania to €0.2452/kWh in Latvia, while grid carbon intensity spans 110 gCO₂/kWh in Latvia, 146 gCO₂/kWh in Lithuania, and 460 gCO₂/kWh in Estonia. Those spreads shape economics and emissions, but they do not tell you how deep EPREL-listed supply is in each country.

The broader EPREL context is highly concentrated. Daikin Europe N.V. alone accounts for 14,668 models, or 24.05% of the full catalogue. Mitsubishi Electric Europe follows at 5,575 models (9.14%), and Johnson Controls Hitachi at 5,207 (8.54%). In a small Baltic subset, that kind of upstream concentration can make local catalogs look more skewed than they really are. The same pattern appears on the product side: [air-water heat pumps](https://househeating-pulse.com/heat-pumps?type=air-water) dominate the registry at 30,452 models, while [water-water](https://househeating-pulse.com/heat-pumps?type=water-water) systems are rare at just 31.

Refrigerant declarations point in the same direction. R32 is the clear volume leader with 13,935 declarations, far ahead of R410A at 1,896 and R290 at 537.
