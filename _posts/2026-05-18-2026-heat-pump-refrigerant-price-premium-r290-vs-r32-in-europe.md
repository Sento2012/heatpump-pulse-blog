The most important finding is also the simplest: **R290 is visible in Europe’s heat-pump market, but it is still a niche offer set**, so any “green premium” has to be understood as a scarcity effect first and a performance story second. In the EPREL-backed snapshot, the catalog contains **60,989 models** in total, but only **537 R290 listings** versus **13,935 R32 listings**. That puts R290 at about **0.88%** of all listed models, while R32 accounts for roughly **22.85%**. Natural refrigerants overall remain a small segment at **3.27%** of the catalog.

That matters because it changes how price comparisons should be read. A premium for R290 is not being set in a mature, dense market; it is being formed in a thin slice of the available product universe. The full analysis on [househeating-pulse.com](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-refrigerant-price-premium-r290-vs-r32-in-europe) breaks down why that scarcity matters more than the marketing language around refrigerants.

The data also weakens the idea that refrigerant choice cleanly explains efficiency. The market-wide average SCOP is **4.55**, while the clearest efficiency differences in the registry are by system type: **6.15** for water-water, **4.77** for ground-water, and **4.54** for air-water. By contrast, the supplied corpus does **not** include refrigerant-level SCOP or price fields, so it cannot prove that R290 commands a higher price because it delivers a higher seasonal efficiency.

The regulatory backdrop still helps explain demand pressure. R290 is listed with **GWP 0**, while R32 carries a **GWP of 771** and an EU phase-out date of **2027-01-01** in the supplied schedule. That asymmetry is likely to keep R290 strategically attractive even if the catalog remains small.
