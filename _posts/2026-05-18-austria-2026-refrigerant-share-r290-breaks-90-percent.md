Austria’s heat-pump catalog has crossed a structural threshold: **R290 now accounts for 90.6% of listed models**, which means propane is no longer a fringe option but the default refrigerant in the national listing mix. That is the key signal, because it shows a market that has already moved beyond “transition” and into dominance at the product-catalog level.

The contrast with the wider EU picture is sharp. Across the European dataset, **R290 represents just 537 of 60,989 models, or 0.88%**, while **R32 sits at 22.8%** and **R410A at 3.1%**. In other words, Austria is not merely ahead of the curve; it is operating with a refrigerant composition that looks qualitatively different from the continental baseline. The full breakdown is here: [Austria 2026: R290 tops 90% of heat-pump listings](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/austria-2026-refrigerant-share-r290-breaks-90-percent).

That matters for more than taxonomy. Once a refrigerant clears nine in ten listings, it starts shaping procurement, installer familiarity, stocking assumptions, and policy expectations. The technical difference is also material: R290 is propane with **GWP 0** and **A3** flammability classification, while R32 remains an HFC with **GWP 771** and an **A2L** class. Older gases are still visible in Europe, but Austria’s catalog implies they are becoming secondary rather than standard.

The broader market context reinforces the point. The EU snapshot includes **60,989 listed models from 777 manufacturers**, with an average SCOP of **4.55**. Austria’s standout feature is not documented efficiency leadership; it is the speed and completeness of refrigerant substitution.
