R290 is no longer the odd one out in Europe’s heat-pump listings; the market question has shifted to whether buyers are paying materially more for it than for R32, and where that premium can actually be recovered.

In the 2026 EU index, R290 appears in **537 listings** out of **60,989 models**, while R32 shows **13,935 listings**. That still leaves propane far behind on raw breadth, but it is now visible enough to influence procurement and catalog filtering rather than sitting at the margins. The broader natural-refrigerant bucket is only **3.27%** of the database, so R290 is carrying most of that visibility in the current marketplace. The full dataset is here: [https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-share-breakeven-rise](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-share-breakeven-rise)

The structural gap versus R32 is not just count-based. R290 listings in the visible top-power set are concentrated in small units: the largest shown is a **6.0 kW** Mitsubishi Electric model, and most others fall in the **2.8–4.1 kW** range. By contrast, R32 spans much larger capacities, with top listings reaching **89.0 kW** and several models above **40 kW**. That means the two refrigerants are not yet competing head-to-head across the same product architecture; R290 is still concentrated in a narrower segment where direct substitutes are fewer.

Pricing is the missing variable in the corpus, but the economics frame is clear. For a system with roughly **SCOP 4**, the tariff ratio has to be favorable for running-cost savings to offset a higher sticker price. Among the countries covered, **France, the Netherlands, Portugal, and Sweden** are the strongest environments for that case, with electricity-to-gas ratios below the breakeven threshold.

R290 has crossed the visibility threshold. The next constraint is not awareness, but whether the market can expand capacity range and close the price gap. **Read the full analysis with live data at the canonical URL.**
