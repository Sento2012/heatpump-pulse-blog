The Dutch heat-pump market in 2026 is being shaped more by **fuel-price economics** than by subsidy generosity. The key signal is an electricity-to-gas ratio of **1.49** — the **second-lowest** in Europe — which sits far below the rough **3.7** parity threshold often used for a **SCOP 4** heat pump. In other words, Dutch households can already see favorable running costs before grants enter the picture.

That matters because the headline subsidy is not especially large. Under ISDE, the maximum support for a full air-water unit is **€2,750**, compared with much higher ceilings in other markets such as **Germany (€21,000)**, **Austria (€23,000)**, **Poland (€31,000)** and **France (€11,000)**. Yet the Dutch tariff structure still gives heat pumps a strong operational case: electricity is about **€0.2558/kWh**, while gas is **€0.1719/kWh**. At a SCOP of 4, that implies delivered heat costs near **€0.064/kWh** from electricity versus gas at the retail tariff level, before boiler efficiency is even considered.

The wider European context reinforces why the Netherlands stands out. Only **Sweden (1.30)** is better on the electricity-to-gas ratio, while **France (1.78)** and **Portugal (1.73)** are the next closest major comparators. Dutch climate conditions are not unusual — the country has about **2,901 HDD₁₈** — and its grid carbon intensity is middling at **268 gCO₂/kWh**, so the advantage is not driven by weather or exceptionally clean power.

On the supply side, the market looks broad but concentrated. The current EU snapshot includes **60,989** models from **777** manufacturers, with **air-water** units dominating at **30,452** listings. The leading brand, **Daikin**, accounts for **24.05%** of listed models, while the top five brands together reach **51.93%**.
