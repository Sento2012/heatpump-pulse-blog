Germany’s R290 story is best read as a **brand-concentration signal**, not yet as proof of a broad market conversion. The hard constraint is data: the corpus does **not** include a Germany-only refrigerant-by-brand table, so the exact share of German R290 listings by top manufacturers cannot be stated. What it does show is that propane remains a small part of the wider EPREL universe: **537 R290 listings out of 16,443 declared refrigerant entries**, or **3.26%**. By comparison, **R32 accounts for 84.75%** and **R410A for 11.53%**.

That matters because a refrigerant shift can look larger than it is when the underlying lineup is top-heavy. Across all listed heat-pump models, **Daikin** holds **24.05%** of the market, with **Mitsubishi Electric** at **9.14%**, **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **8.54%**, **Bosch** at **5.91%**, and **Ariston** at **4.29%**. The top five brands together exceed **51.9%** of listed models. In a structure like that, a propane pivot by one or two large OEMs can move the headline faster than dozens of smaller suppliers adopting later.

Germany is a particularly important test case because policy is actively nudging the chemistry mix: the BEG EM subsidy adds a **5 percentage-point bonus** for natural refrigerants or ground/water source systems, on top of a **30% base rate**, with a **70% cap** and **€30,000** eligible-cost ceiling. With electricity at **€0.3869/kWh** and gas at **€0.1223/kWh**, refrigerant choice is part of the commercial equation, not just a technical detail.

The practical takeaway is simple: if R290 is expanding in Germany, the first question is which brands are pulling it, not whether the entire market has already shifted.
