Belgium’s 2026 heat-pump economics are tight enough that tariff timing still matters. On the latest household data, electricity sits at **€0.3499/kWh** while gas is **€0.0898/kWh**, giving an electricity-to-gas ratio of **3.9**. That is above the rough **3.7** break-even line often used for a heat pump with **SCOP 4**, so the standard Belgian tariff no longer offers much cushion.

The practical implication is simple: a SCOP-4 unit is only marginally ahead on energy cost at the blended household tariff. Delivered heat from the heat pump costs about **€0.0875/kWh-heat** at today’s electricity price, versus **€0.0898/kWh** for gas fuel input before boiler losses. That gap is only around **€0.0023/kWh of heat**, which means annual savings remain small unless the load can be shifted into cheaper hours. At **10,000 kWh** of annual heat demand, that is roughly **€23/year** on a simplified comparison.

Belgium also ranks near the top of the European price-ratio table: **24th out of 26** countries, with only the **UK** and **Romania** higher. That matters because Belgium’s constraint is not especially expensive gas; it is the relatively high electricity price. In other words, the market structure pushes the economics of heat pumps toward control strategy, not just hardware efficiency.

The key missing variable is the exact current Belgian day-night split. The available dataset does not include the country’s present-night tariff or the discount versus daytime power, so the precise savings from running a heat pump overnight cannot be verified here. But the threshold is clear: if the night rate falls materially below about **€0.332/kWh**, the economics start to improve meaningfully for SCOP-4 systems.
