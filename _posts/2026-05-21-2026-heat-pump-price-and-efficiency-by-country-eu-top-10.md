Europe’s heat-pump value equation in 2026 is being shaped more by tariff ratios and subsidy ceilings than by a simple “cheap country vs expensive country” split. Across the top 10 markets, the decisive question is whether electricity is still low enough relative to gas for a heat pump to beat combustion on running cost. In this comparison, the Netherlands (1.49) and France (1.78) are the clearest operating-cost winners, while Belgium (3.90) and Poland (3.71) sit at or above the rough 3.7 breakpoint where a SCOP 4 system stops looking automatically better than gas without support.

That matters because model availability is broad but not evenly useful. The EU-wide registry now covers 60,989 models from 777 manufacturers, with an average SCOP of 4.55. Most buyers are still shopping in air-to-water systems, which average 4.54 across 30,452 listings — essentially flat with the overall market. By contrast, ground-water units average 4.77, and water-water reaches 6.15, but with only 31 models listed it remains niche. The [full country-by-country analysis](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-price-and-efficiency-by-country-eu-top-10) breaks down how those product realities interact with national support and energy prices.

Subsidy design is the other major divider. Poland offers the largest maximum support in the group at €31,000, ahead of Austria (€23,000) and Germany (€21,000). At the opposite end, the Netherlands tops out at just €2,750, with Spain at €3,000 and Belgium at €4,000. That creates a large gap in effective upfront cost, even before installer pricing is considered.

For analysts, the conclusion is straightforward: France and the Netherlands currently offer the strongest operating economics, Poland offers the deepest support but weak tariff conditions, and Belgium is the hardest market to justify on pure running-cost grounds.
