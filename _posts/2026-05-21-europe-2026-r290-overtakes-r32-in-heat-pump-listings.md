## R290 is gaining attention, but R32 still dominates the EPREL corpus

The main data point is not an outright refrigerant crossover. In the current EPREL-derived snapshot, **R32 still accounts for 13,935 listings**, while consolidated **R290-coded entries total 540** when spelling variants are grouped. That leaves R290 far behind, even though regulatory momentum is clearly moving in its direction.

The reason this story matters is the widening gap between market signal and market structure. **R290 has GWP 0**, while **R32 is listed at GWP 771** and carries an **F-gas phase-out date of 2027-01-01**. **R410A** is already under even tighter pressure, with **GWP 1,924** and a **2025-01-01 phase-out date**. So the product direction is obvious; the deployment curve is not.

On share terms, the corpus still looks heavily locked into legacy refrigerants. **R32 covers about 22.85% of all 60,989 listed models**, while consolidated **R290 sits below 1%**. That is not a completed transition; it is an early-stage portfolio shift.

The market is also concentrated enough that any real change will likely come from a small number of large manufacturers rather than the long tail. **Daikin Europe N.V. alone lists 14,668 models**, or **24.05%** of the database, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. at 5,575** and **Johnson Controls Hitachi Europe at 5,207**. In other words, refrigerant mix changes will be decided in large catalogs, not fringe ones.

Economics reinforce the split. Countries with the most favorable electricity-to-gas ratios include **Sweden (1.30)**, **the Netherlands (1.49)**, **France (1.78)** and **Italy (2.00)**, while **Germany (3.16)** and the **UK (4.63)** remain much less supportive. That does not prove where R290 is growing fastest, but it does show where the policy and operating-cost backdrop is most aligned with lower-GWP heat pumps.
