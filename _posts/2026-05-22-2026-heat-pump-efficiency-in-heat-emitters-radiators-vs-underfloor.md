The key finding is narrower than the usual radiator-vs-flooring debate suggests: emitter choice affects the flow temperature a system can sustain, but EPREL data shows that **heat-pump type and national tariff structure explain more of the real-world economics than emitters alone**.

Across the current EPREL-linked catalog, declared seasonal efficiency varies sharply by source type. **Air-water units average SCOP 4.54 across 30,452 models**, while **ground-water reaches 4.77 across 213 models** and **water-water averages 6.15 across just 31 models**. That 1.61-point spread from air-water to water-water is large enough to matter before a buyer even gets to radiator sizing or floor loops. The same pattern appears on acoustics: average outdoor sound power is **59.8 dB for air-water** versus **42.0 dB for water-water**, a 17.8 dB gap — but that is a source-type effect, not proof that underfloor heating itself makes a unit quieter.

For retrofit planning, the practical issue is whether radiators can run low enough to let a heat pump stay in a favourable operating band. Underfloor heating is the cleaner match because it is designed around lower temperatures, which is why it more naturally supports higher SCOP outcomes. Still, the catalog also shows that high performance is not confined to one niche: the top declared SCOP in the dataset is **7.0**, with several models clustered at **6.97** and **6.95**.

The market context matters just as much. Using the article’s break-even threshold, a heat pump with SCOP 4 becomes competitive against gas when the electricity-to-gas price ratio approaches **3.7**. In the current cross-country snapshot, **all 25 countries with both tariff inputs sit below that line**, with **France at 1.78**, **Germany at 3.16**, and **Belgium at 3.90** as the only market above it.
