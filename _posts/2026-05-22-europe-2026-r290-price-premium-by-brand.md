The key shift is no longer *whether* European heat-pump lines include R290, but whether brands have priced propane as a mainstream option or left it as a premium tier. The market still shows a wide adoption gap: 537 declared R290 models versus 13,935 R32 models, or roughly 3.9% as many. But the sharper commercial question is how that refrigerant choice maps to list price once you compare manufacturers side by side.

That pricing layer is exactly where the public snapshot becomes more revealing structurally, even if the full brand-by-brand price matrix is still not exposed in the excerpted dataset. Europe’s model universe now spans 60,989 heat pumps from 777 manufacturers, and natural refrigerants account for 3.27% of all listed models. In other words, propane is present at scale, but it is still a small minority within the broader catalog. The live market snapshot on [Househeating Pulse](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-price-premium-by-brand) shows why that matters: adoption alone no longer tells you whether R290 is competitively priced.

Supplier scale also changes the stakes. Daikin Europe N.V. leads the snapshot with 14,668 models and 24.05% share, followed by Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. at 5,575 models and 9.14%, and Johnson Controls Hitachi at 5,207 models and 8.54%. Bosch Thermotechnik and Ariston round out the next tier at 3,602 and 2,618 models. Together, the top 10 brands hold 60.93% of the catalog, so any propane premium or discount concentrated among them would materially affect the European market.

The full analysis also separates refrigerant choice from performance claims. Among large brands, average SCOP ranges from Bosch’s 4.69 to Hitachi’s 4.18, a spread of 0.51 points, yet the dataset does not support using efficiency alone to justify R290 markups.
