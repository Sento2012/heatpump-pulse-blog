Southern and Nordic heat-pump buyers are not facing the same economics, but the registry does **not** support a clean country-by-country price ranking. The sharper signal is structural: running cost, power-system carbon, and product mix matter more than headline sticker price, and the EPREL corpus only partially exposes those variables. The full analysis is here: https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-prices-and-efficiency-in-europe-southern-vs-nordic-markets

On running costs, the electricity-to-gas ratio is low enough in both regions to keep heat pumps competitive. Portugal is the strongest southern case at **1.73**, followed by Italy at **2.0**, Spain at **2.79**, Greece at **2.59**, and Croatia at **3.05**. In the colder set, Sweden is the clear outlier at just **1.3**, while Denmark sits at **2.63**, Estonia at **3.03**, Latvia at **2.97**, and Lithuania at **2.86**. None of the listed southern or Nordic markets with gas data crosses the rough **3.7** break-even threshold often used for a SCOP 4 system.

The wider European product universe is still dominated by air-to-water units: **30,452 of 60,989 models**, or **49.9%**, with air-air at **34.5%**. Ground-water is only **0.35%** of the indexed market, and water-water just **0.05%**. That concentration matters because the highest-efficiency end of the market is not all exotic systems; the top SCOP set is **11 air-water models and 4 water-water models**, with the leading entry reaching **SCOP 7.0**.

Refrigerant choice also signals the next buying cycle. R290 appears in **537 listings** — about **0.88%** of the total universe — while R32 remains dominant at **13,935 listings**. Natural refrigerants are still a small share overall, but they are becoming part of the premium and future-proofing discussion.
