R290 is expanding in Europe’s heat-pump listings, but the market is still far from a clean propane transition. In the current EPREL-based snapshot, R290 appears on 537 models out of 16,444 refrigerant-coded listings, or about 3.3%. By contrast, R32 still accounts for roughly 84.7% and R410A about 11.5%. The key point: adoption is real, but it is not yet the dominant regime.

That matters because the market is structurally concentrated. Househeating Pulse’s index covers 60,989 heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers, and the top 15 brands alone represent about 67% of listings. Daikin Europe N.V. is the largest single manufacturer at 24.05% of all models, followed by Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. at 9.14% and Johnson Controls Hitachi Air Conditioning Europe at 8.54%. When a handful of OEMs control so much of the catalog, even modest changes in their refrigerant strategy can move the market narrative quickly.

The more interesting question is pricing: does R290 carry a premium, and is that premium consistent across brands? The analysis argues that the answer cannot be generalized from the available corpus. The data set supports market-size and manufacturer-share comparisons, but not a brand-by-brand price test for propane versus non-propane models. That means claims about “premium R290” versus “commodity R290” should be treated cautiously unless they are backed by model-level pricing evidence.

Efficiency patterns also avoid a simple refrigerant story. The market-wide average SCOP is 4.55, but brand averages vary materially: Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH reaches 4.69, Ariston SpA 4.66, and Gree Spain Corporation 4.65, while Daikin averages 4.44 despite its dominant market share.
