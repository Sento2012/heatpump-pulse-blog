R32 is the dominant refrigerant in the current EPREL snapshot, and that fact matters more than any assumed efficiency narrative. In a registry of **60,989** heat-pump models from **777 manufacturers**, **13,935** listings are tagged R32, versus only **537** for R290. That’s a **22.85%** share for R32 and just **0.88%** for R290 — roughly a **26:1** listing imbalance. The market is not comparing two equally sized pools; it is comparing a mainstream refrigerant to a niche one.

That distinction also limits what can be proven from the data. The EPREL corpus used here does **not** expose refrigerant-split price fields, so there is no basis for calculating an R290 premium or an R32 discount. It also does not provide a direct refrigerant-by-SCOP table, which means the common claim that one refrigerant “buys” higher efficiency cannot be quantified from this dataset alone. The broader benchmark, however, is clear: average SCOP across all refrigerants is **4.77** for ground-water units, **4.54** for air-water, and **6.15** for water-water systems.

The available model slices do show that refrigerant choice is entangled with hardware profile. In the smallest-capacity R290 slice, listed outputs range from **0.7 kW** to **3.2 kW**, while the comparable R32 slice runs from **1.0 kW** to **1.9 kW**. Both are dominated by air-air products, and both contain outdoor-noise figures clustered in a similar band once obvious zero-value anomalies are ignored. That suggests product mix, not refrigerant alone, is driving much of the observed variation.

The practical takeaway is narrow but useful: **R32 offers far more search depth**, while **R290 remains a much smaller segment with a different product mix**. Whether that mix translates into a real efficiency or cost advantage depends on the individual model, not the refrigerant label.
