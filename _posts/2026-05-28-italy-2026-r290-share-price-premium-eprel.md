Italy is still a propane laggard in the visible EPREL catalog, but the gap is best understood as structural rather than purely technical. In the current live market snapshot, **R290 appears on 3.27% of listed models**, while **R32 accounts for 22.85%**. That means propane is present, but it is still far from the dominant refrigerant choice in the public listing universe.

The more important signal is that the market is moving in the right direction: R290’s regulatory position is materially stronger than R32’s. In the reference table, **R290 is assigned GWP 0**, while **R32 carries GWP 771** and a **2027 phase-out date**. For manufacturers planning 2026–2027 product portfolios, that asymmetry matters more each quarter, even if Italy’s listing mix has not yet fully shifted.

Pricing remains the open question. The article’s central thesis is that the **premium on R290 is narrowing**, but the underlying dataset does **not** provide Italy-specific R290-vs-R32 price pairs or a refrigerant-level SCOP comparison, so the exact spread cannot be verified from this snapshot alone. What the broader country context does show is why price sensitivity remains high: Italian households pay **€0.2966/kWh for electricity versus €0.1481/kWh for gas**, a **2.00x** ratio that raises the bar for any higher upfront-cost heat pump.

That makes Italy a market where policy, regulation, and operating economics are pulling in different directions. Support exists — notably **Conto Termico**, with aid up to **€5,000** or **65% of eligible cost** — but subsidy coverage does not automatically erase a higher list price.
