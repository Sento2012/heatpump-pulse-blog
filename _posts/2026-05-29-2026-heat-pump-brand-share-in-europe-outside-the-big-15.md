The biggest takeaway is not a special “rest-of-Europe” brand map — it’s that the full EPREL catalog is already so concentrated that smaller markets are likely to inherit the same hierarchy. Europe-wide, the database contains **60,989 models** from **777 manufacturers**, yet **Daikin Europe N.V.** alone accounts for **14,668 listings** or **24.05%** of all entries. The next two brands, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** and **Hitachi/Johnson Controls**, are far behind at **9.14%** and **8.54%**, which means the **top five brands already hold 51.93%** of listings. See the full benchmark here: https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-brand-share-in-europe-outside-the-big-15

That concentration matters because the corpus does not expose a separate EPREL cut for “Europe outside the big 15.” What it does show is a market with a long tail, but a very steep front end: the **top 10 brands reach 60.88%**, while the remaining **757 manufacturers** share the rest. For smaller-country buyers, that implies brand availability will probably be shaped more by the same large groups than by truly local players.

The product mix is similarly skewed toward mainstream categories. Across Europe, **air-water units make up 49.9%** of listings and **air-air units 34.5%**; niche hydronic categories are tiny, with **ground-water at 0.35%** and **water-water at 0.05%**. Efficiency varies by type, but the overall structure is clear: the catalog is built around mainstream, high-volume formats rather than specialist systems.

Economics outside the biggest markets are often more favorable than the public debate suggests. In the tariff table, **Bulgaria** posts an electricity-to-gas ratio of **2.09**, **Slovenia** **2.44**, and **Czechia** **3.35** — all below the rough **3.7** break-even guide for a SCOP 4 heat pump. That means running-cost logic can work even in cooler countries, not just in southern Europe.
