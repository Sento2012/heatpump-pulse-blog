France’s heat-pump case is being shaped less by today’s flat retail price than by the gap between electricity and gas. The latest household figures put power at **€0.2561/kWh** and gas at **€0.1436/kWh**, a ratio of **1.78** — well below the rough **3.7** break-even marker often used for a **SCOP 4** heat pump versus a gas boiler. That means efficiency still matters more than fuel-price parity in France.

The broader price history is mixed. Electricity has eased from its **2024-H2** peak of **€0.2926/kWh** to **€0.2561/kWh** today, a drop of about **12.5%**. But it remains far above the **2018-H1** low of **€0.1748/kWh**, so “cheap power” is not the right framing. On the generation side, the current market-wide average heat-pump **SCOP of 4.55** implies a delivered heat cost of roughly **€0.0563/kWh** at the current French electricity tariff, which is materially below direct electric heating and still competitive with retail gas on an input-price basis.

The catch: the dataset does **not** include France-specific day and night household tariffs, so the actual off-peak advantage cannot be quantified here. That matters because the article’s core question is not whether France has a good average tariff, but whether time shifting still creates a meaningful running-cost edge for flexible heat-pump operation. The available evidence can only say that a flat-rate assumption is a reasonable baseline — not a test of real tariff optimisation.

In European context, France sits near the middle on electricity price but low on the power-to-gas ratio, behind countries like **Poland (3.71)** and the **UK (4.63)** on that metric. For buyers, the implication is structural: tariff shape, not just tariff level, will decide how much value load shifting can add.
