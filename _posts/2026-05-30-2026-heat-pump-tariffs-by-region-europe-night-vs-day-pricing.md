# 2026 heat-pump tariffs in Europe: the key signal is the spread, not just the average

The most useful 2026 finding is not that electricity is “high” or “low” in Europe, but that the tariff gap is large enough to materially change heat-pump operating economics by country. In the current dataset, Ireland sits at **€0.4042/kWh**, while Hungary is at **€0.1082/kWh** — a difference of **€0.2960/kWh**. For the same machine, same weather, and same SCOP, that alone can move annual running costs by hundreds of euros.

That spread matters even more once heating-flexible households start looking at load shifting. The corpus does **not** publish separate day and night rates by country, so there is no ranked Europe-wide table of off-peak discounts here. But the logic is straightforward: if a market is already expensive on headline electricity, any dual-rate contract has a bigger euro impact. That is why countries like **Germany (€0.3869/kWh)**, **Belgium (€0.3499/kWh)**, **Denmark (€0.3312/kWh)** and **Ireland** are the ones where timing optimization is most likely to move the needle.

The tariff picture also lines up with the electricity-to-gas test. Using a SCOP 4 screen, **22 markets** still stay below the roughly **3.7** electricity/gas ratio where heat pumps remain competitive on running cost. Examples include **Sweden at 1.3**, **France at 1.78**, and **Italy at 2.0**. At the other end, **Belgium reaches 3.9**, **the UK 4.63**, and **Romania 5.11**.

For buyers, the practical takeaway is to treat tariff structure as a system input, not a footnote. Efficient hardware, buffer tanks, and pre-heating strategies are most relevant where off-peak electricity is meaningfully cheaper than daytime supply.
