## Europe’s 2026 heat-pump market is not organized the way many buyers assume

The clearest signal from the latest EPREL readout is that **market size and efficiency ranking are pulling in different directions**. The largest category is **air-to-water**, with **30,452 models** — about **49.9%** of the listed universe — but it does **not** lead on average efficiency. In the 2026 snapshot, the best-reported class average belongs to **water-water** at **6.15 SCOP**, followed by **ground-water** at **4.77**, then **air-water** at **4.54**. That puts the mainstream air-to-water segment almost exactly on the overall market average, while the smaller source-side categories sit above it.

That matters because the product mix is heavily skewed toward installable, mass-market formats, not toward the most efficient ones. **Ground-water** represents only **213 models** and **water-water just 31**, so the top performers are structurally rare. By contrast, air-to-air is large too, with **21,065 models** or roughly **34.5%** of the catalog, but the source data does **not** provide a class-average SCOP for that group, so claims that it now beats air-to-water on efficiency cannot be verified from this corpus alone. The strongest data-backed conclusion is simpler: the 2026 hierarchy among types with reported averages is water-water, then ground-water, then air-water.

The practical takeaway is that efficiency alone does not define the buyer decision. Water-water units average **35.65 kW** and **42.0 dB** outdoor noise, while air-water averages **11.83 kW** and **59.8 dB**. So the highest-efficiency classes are also larger and, in the water-water case, quieter — a reminder that type labels bundle capacity, acoustic profile, and application envelope, not just SCOP.
