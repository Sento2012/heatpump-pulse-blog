Belgium’s 2026 heat-pump catalog still looks more like an R32 market than an R290 market. In the EPREL snapshot used here, **R32 appears on 13,935 models**, while **R290 appears on just 537** — a gap of **13,398 listings** and **21.97 percentage points** in share terms. That means roughly **26 R32 models for every 1 R290 model**, which is a very different transition profile from the “propane-first” direction often assumed in Europe.

The broader dataset reinforces how concentrated this is. Across **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers**, the average **SCOP is 4.55**, while **natural refrigerants account for only 3.27%** of the snapshot. R32 alone holds **22.85%** of all listed models, compared with **0.88% for R290**. In other words, the refrigerant mix is still overwhelmingly shaped by legacy HFC and A2L portfolios, not by a rapid switch to propane.

Brand concentration matters here. The top five suppliers — **Daikin Europe N.V. (24.05%)**, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. (9.14%)**, **Johnson Controls Hitachi (8.54%)**, **Bosch Thermotechnik (5.91%)**, and **Ariston (4.29%)** — control **51.93%** of all listed models. The top ten reach **60.88%**. That level of concentration means the pace of refrigerant transition is being set by a small number of portfolio decisions, not by the long tail of smaller brands.

Efficiency does not cleanly map to refrigerant choice. The top-brand SCOP range runs from **3.93 at Toshiba Carrier** to **4.69 at Bosch**, with several major brands above the 4.55 market average and several below it. The structural message is clearer than the technology narrative: Belgium’s slow move away from R32 is primarily a portfolio-composition issue.
