Austria is now one of Europe’s clearest signal markets for the refrigerant transition: **more than 90% of listed heat pumps use R290**, while **R32 has been pushed into the low single digits**. That matters because Austria is no longer a case study in whether propane is viable; it is a case study in **which brands completed the switch and which one still has not**.

The structural backdrop is supportive. Household electricity is **€0.3272/kWh** versus gas at **€0.1221/kWh**, an **electricity-to-gas ratio of 2.68**. For a typical heat pump operating around **SCOP 4**, that remains comfortably below the rough break-even ratio often used against gas boilers, so the economics do not force a compromise back toward legacy refrigerants. Austria is also backed by policy: the federal **“Raus aus Öl”** programme can reach **€23,000** in support, with a **75% cap** for eligible low-income households.

The broader European catalog shows how unusual Austria is. Across the full market index, **R32 still dominates with 13,935 models**, while **R290 has 537** and natural refrigerants overall account for just **3.27%** of listed models. In other words, Austria is not following the European average; it is moving far ahead of it. For reference, the full market average SCOP sits at **4.55**, but Austria-specific performance by refrigerant is not disclosed in the source data.

The real market question is now brand composition. Large groups such as **Daikin Europe N.V. (14,668 models)**, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. (5,575)**, and **Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH (3,602)** have the scale to shape how quickly national catalogs flip to propane. Yet one manufacturer still appears tied to R32 more than its peers, creating a visible holdout inside an otherwise R290-heavy market.
