R290 has crossed from transition technology to the largest refrigerant block in Europe’s live heat-pump listings, but the shift is not uniform and the underlying declaration base still looks very different. In the current market snapshot, **R290 holds 53.7% of listings versus 46.3% for R32**, a **7.4-point lead**. Yet in the broader EPREL declaration universe, **R32 appears 13,935 times** while **R290 shows only 537 entries** — a sign that the live offer set is moving faster than the legacy catalog.

That split matters for anyone trying to read Europe’s refrigerant transition as a single trend. The live index suggests propane is already the dominant commercial choice, but the registry data still reflects a market shaped by older HFC-heavy portfolios. The practical implication is that adoption is being driven by what manufacturers are actively promoting now, not by the full historical record of declared models.

The country backdrop helps explain why the transition is uneven. Household electricity-to-gas ratios range from **1.3 in Sweden** to **5.11 in Romania**, with the **Netherlands at 1.49**, **France at 1.78**, and **Germany at 3.16**. That tariff spread changes the operating-cost case for heat pumps before equipment pricing is even considered. On the policy side, subsidy ceilings also vary widely, from **€2,750 in the Netherlands** to **€31,000 in Poland** among countries with active programmes.

Brand structure is another clue. Europe’s listing base is still concentrated: **Daikin Europe N.V.** holds **14,668 models** and **24.05%** of listings, while **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** has **5,575** and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** **5,207**. Together, those three account for **41.73%** of the market. That concentration tends to slow or accelerate refrigerant change depending on portfolio strategy.
