Sweden is emerging as a leading indicator for Europe’s refrigerant transition: if R290 listings have now moved above 50% there, the market has crossed from mixed adoption into majority propane territory. That would place Sweden far ahead of the continental baseline, where R290 still represents only **537 of 60,989** listed models, or **0.88%** of EPREL entries. By contrast, **R32** remains the dominant refrigerant across Europe with **13,935 listings** (**22.85%**), while **R410A** still appears in **1,896 models** (**3.11%**).

That gap matters because it implies Sweden is not simply tracking the European average; it is likely acting as a structural outlier. In the broader database, “natural refrigerant” products are still only **3.27%** of all listed models. A Swedish R290 majority would therefore signal a portfolio shift that is well ahead of the continent-wide product mix, not a reflection of it.

The commercial backdrop is less supportive than the listing trend. Sweden’s electricity-to-gas ratio is **1.3**, well below the roughly **3.7** break-even benchmark often used for a SCOP 4 heat pump versus a gas boiler. It also has **no active heat-pump subsidy** in the register, unlike Austria, which combines a **2.68** tariff ratio with up to **€23,000** in support. Sweden’s grid carbon intensity is a major counterweight at just **14 gCO₂/kWh**, but the price signal alone does not explain a propane-led catalog.

For manufacturers and installers, the implication is clear: Sweden may be becoming a lead market for R290 portfolio localization, especially in mainstream **air-to-water** products, where most European listings already sit.
