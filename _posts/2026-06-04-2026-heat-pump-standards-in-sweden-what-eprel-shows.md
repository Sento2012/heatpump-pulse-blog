Sweden’s heat-pump market looks less like an emerging niche and more like a mature, efficiency-led catalog. In the EPREL snapshot used here, **80.73% of listed models are A+ or better**, and **53.12% sit in A++ or A+++**. That matters in a country with **4,242.38 heating degree days**, where electrified heating is structurally advantaged and buyers are already choosing from a high-efficiency baseline. Full data context is here: [https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-standards-in-sweden-what-eprel-shows](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-standards-in-sweden-what-eprel-shows)

The catalog is also large and concentrated. Househeating Pulse’s EPREL index records **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers** in the Sweden-relevant shopping universe. The top three suppliers — **Daikin Europe N.V.**, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.**, and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** — account for **41.73%** of listings combined, while Daikin alone holds **24.05%**. That concentration suggests that model choice, brand strategy, and refrigerant selection may influence the shortlist as much as nominal efficiency does.

Type mix reinforces that point. **Air-water units make up 49.93%** of listings and **air-air units 34.54%**, leaving ground-source categories as small minorities. Efficiency is highest in the water-water segment at **6.15 SCOP**, but the segment is tiny at just **31 models**. By contrast, mainstream air-water units average **4.54 SCOP** across **30,452 models**, showing that most of the catalog clusters around practical, not extreme, performance.

Refrigerants show a similar pattern. **R32 appears on 13,935 listings**, or **84.77%** of declared refrigerant-coded models, while **R290 is only 3.27%**. That means the transition to lower-GWP refrigerants is visible, but not yet dominant in the searchable market.

The key takeaway: Sweden’s EPREL landscape is already highly efficient, but the structure of the market — brand concentration, type mix, and refrigerant choice — is still doing a lot of the sorting before price even enters the picture.
