The clearest signal in EPREL is not that R290 is growing, but that adoption is still highly uneven: across 60,989 listed heat pumps from 777 manufacturers, only 537 models use propane. That puts R290 at 3.27% of the catalog, while R32 alone appears in 13,935 listings and R410A still exceeds propane by 1,359 models. The market is shifting, but the center of gravity remains fluorinated refrigerants.

That matters because the transition is not distributed evenly across the catalog. Air-water systems make up 30,452 listings and air-air 21,065, with 9,228 heat-pump water heaters on top; ground-water and water-water products are tiny by comparison at 213 and 31 models. Any refrigerant move concentrated in one segment can distort the headline picture, which is why the aggregate share only tells part of the story. The detailed EPREL view is here: [househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-gap-in-heat-pump-listings-by-brand](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-gap-in-heat-pump-listings-by-brand).

The brand structure is equally important. Daikin Europe N.V. is the largest catalog owner with 14,668 models, or 24.05% of all listings. Mitsubishi Electric Europe follows with 5,575 models (9.14%), and Johnson Controls Hitachi Air Conditioning Europe with 5,207 (8.54%). Combined, the top three control 41.73% of the EPREL catalog. That concentration means any large manufacturer that leans harder into R290 can move the market faster than its raw unit count would suggest.

The article also shows why refrigerant mix cannot be inferred from scale alone. High catalog share does not automatically mean high propane share, and the current dataset does not provide a brand-by-brand refrigerant split. What is measurable is that the market-wide R290 base is still small enough that any manufacturer materially above 3.27% would be well ahead of the pack.
