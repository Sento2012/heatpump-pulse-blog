The clearest efficiency signal in the 2026 EPREL snapshot is not “R290 everywhere,” but **R290 inside air-to-water systems**. That segment is both the largest comparable category and the one where the refrigerant split is most pronounced, making it the main battleground for performance comparisons between R290 and R32.

The underlying market structure explains why. EPREL in this snapshot includes **60,989** listed heat-pump models, but only three product types carry SCOP data here: **30,452 air-to-water units**, **213 ground-water units**, and **31 water-water units**. By contrast, **21,065 air-air** models and **9,228 heat-pump water heaters** are present in the broader catalog, but not in the SCOP-comparable set used for this analysis. That means claims about “the whole market” are too broad; the data support a type-specific reading.

Air-to-water also sits near the center of the market on non-refrigerant metrics: its average **SCOP is 4.54**, close to the overall snapshot average of **4.55**, with average declared capacity of **11.83 kW** and outdoor noise of **59.8 dB**. That profile matters because it is the category most buyers and installers actually cross-shop, so even a modest efficiency advantage has meaningful operating-cost implications.

The contrast with other types is sharp. **Ground-water** reaches a higher average SCOP of **4.77**, but with only **213** models it is a niche. **Water-water** is higher still at **6.15 SCOP**, but the category is tiny at just **31** models and behaves very differently in size and application. The result is a market where refrigerant choice is most consequential in air-to-water, while elsewhere the signal is either too small or too structurally different to generalize confidently.

R32 remains far more prevalent in the catalog, with **13,935** declared models versus **537** for R290.
