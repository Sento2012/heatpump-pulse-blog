The strongest finding is also the most constrained: the public EPREL snapshot is rich enough to show Europe-wide concentration, but not enough to prove a Baltic-vs-Central-Europe brand ranking from this corpus alone. What *is* clear is that the listing universe is large — 60,989 heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers — and heavily skewed toward a few brands at the top.

Across the full registry, [Daikin Europe N.V.](https://househeating-pulse.com/manufacturers/daikin-europe-n-v) leads with 14,668 models, or 24.05% of all listed products. [Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.](https://househeating-pulse.com/manufacturers/mitsubishi-electric-europe-b-v) follows at 9.14%, and [Johnson Controls Hitachi](https://househeating-pulse.com/manufacturers/johnson-controls-hitachi-air-conditioning-europe-sas-sucursal-en-espana) at 8.54%. Combined, the top three account for 41.73% of the market, while the top five reach 51.93% and the top ten 60.88%. That is a strong concentration signal: a small group of suppliers occupies most of the declared shelf space.

The refrigerant picture points in the same direction. R32 appears on 13,935 listings, equal to 22.85% of the snapshot, while R290 is still small at 537 listings, or 0.88%. Natural refrigerants overall reach 3.27% of the index. For buyers and specifiers, that means the declared catalog still leans toward legacy HFC-based families, even as regulatory pressure changes the mix.

The regional context matters even if the regional brand-share table is missing here. The Baltic states sit above 4,400 heating degree days — Estonia 4,474.47, Lithuania 4,423.05, Latvia 4,407.08 — while several Central European markets are materially lower, such as Austria at 3,309.19 and Germany at 3,308.21. That climate gap can change which brands and product types are operationally relevant.
