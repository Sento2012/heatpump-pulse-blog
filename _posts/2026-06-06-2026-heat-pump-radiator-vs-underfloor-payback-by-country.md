## Radiator vs underfloor changes the payback math more than many retrofit models assume

The headline result is that the same heat pump can move from marginal to attractive simply by changing the emitter system. Underfloor heating tends to preserve lower flow temperatures and keep seasonal efficiency closer to the better end of the range, while radiators often force the machine into a less efficient operating band. In 2026, that matters because the electricity-to-gas price gap is large enough in some countries to reward every SCOP point, but narrow enough in others to punish any efficiency loss.

The country spread is wide. Sweden sits at an electricity-to-gas ratio of **1.3**, France at **1.78**, Germany at **3.16**, Poland at **3.71**, and Romania at **5.11**. A heat pump running at roughly **SCOP 4** is near the break-even zone when electricity costs about **3.7x** gas, so Poland is effectively on the line and Romania is beyond it. That means underfloor retrofits have a clearer payback case in low-ratio markets, while radiator-based systems become much more subsidy-dependent as ratios rise. EPREL data shows a technical backdrop that supports this split. In the current aggregation, **air-water** heat pumps average **SCOP 4.54** across **30,452 models**, while **ground-water** averages **4.77** across **213 models**. That **0.23-point** gap is only about **5.1%**, but in a tariff environment where margins are tight, it is enough to change payback sensitivity once emitter design is added. Across all SCOP-reporting types, **water-water** leads at **6.15**, underscoring how much spread exists in the registry.

The strongest use case combines low price ratios with meaningful heating demand. Sweden’s **4,242 HDD18** and France’s **2,759.65 HDD18** help annual savings accumulate, while Germany and Poland sit in the more delicate middle where controls, subsidies, and emitter temperature matter more than headline pump label values.
