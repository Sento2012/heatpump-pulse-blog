## Poland’s heat-pump economics in 2026 hinge on tariff timing, not just grants

Poland’s latest household electricity benchmark is now **€0.2709/kWh** for **2025 H2**, up from **€0.2559/kWh** in **2025 H1**. That **€0.0150/kWh** step-up matters because it pushes operating-cost optimization closer to the center of the heat-pump decision, especially when the country’s electricity-to-gas ratio sits at **3.71** — essentially on the usual **SCOP 4** break-even line.

The key takeaway is simple: once power is near **€0.27/kWh**, even a moderate off-peak shift can change annual costs by a meaningful amount. The exact 2026 Polish day-rate and night-rate values are not available in the corpus, so the article does not claim a published tariff spread. But the sensitivity is clear: every **€0.01/kWh** moved away from the effective day rate saves **€10 per 1,000 kWh** of heat-pump electricity use. At **€0.05/kWh**, that becomes **€50 per 1,000 kWh shifted**.

That makes load-shifting tools more valuable than headline subsidy figures. Poland’s support ceiling still reaches **€31,000** across programme tiers, but the operating-cost story increasingly depends on how much compressor runtime and hot-water preparation can be moved into cheaper hours. In practical terms, thermal storage, weather compensation, and smart scheduling now matter as much as model selection.

The market context reinforces the point. Poland’s electricity price is no longer low by European standards: it sits above Spain, Luxembourg, and the Netherlands, while remaining below Germany and Belgium. That leaves Poland in the upper-middle band of EU pricing, where tariff structure can materially affect payback.
