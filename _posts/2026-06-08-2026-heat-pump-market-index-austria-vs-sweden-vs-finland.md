Austria, Sweden and Finland all qualify as mature heat-pump markets, but the data show they are mature in **different ways**. The key takeaway is that there is no single “advanced European” operating model: Austria is support-heavy, Sweden is carbon-strong, and Finland is climate-stressed.

On policy, Austria stands apart. It is the only one of the three with an active household heat-pump support scheme in the registry, and its recorded maximum subsidy reaches **€23,000**. Sweden and Finland both show **zero active subsidies** in the same dataset, so the gap in public backing is not marginal.

The operating conditions also diverge sharply. Finland has the highest heating demand at **4,407.92 HDD18**, with Sweden close behind at **4,242.38**, while Austria is much milder at **3,309.19**. That makes Finland the hardest sizing environment and Austria the least climate-stressed of the three. For carbon, the ranking flips in an important way: Sweden’s grid intensity is only **14 gCO₂/kWh**, compared with **79 gCO₂/kWh** in Finland and **89 gCO₂/kWh** in Austria. Sweden therefore combines cold-weather demand with unusually clean electricity, which materially strengthens the emissions case for electrified heating.

The tariff side is less intuitive. Austria’s electricity-to-gas ratio comes out at about **2.68**, Sweden’s at roughly **1.30**, while Finland cannot be scored because the gas price is not recorded. That means Sweden is not the strongest market on pure retail fuel-spread economics, despite its mature heat-pump base.

The companion analysis also places these country differences against the broader EPREL snapshot: **60,989 models**, **777 manufacturers**, and a market still dominated by **air-water** and **air-air** systems. Refrigerant transition is incomplete as well, with **R32** still the largest single refrigerant family and natural refrigerants at only **3.27%** of the indexed universe.
