R290 is still a niche in the EU heat-pump registry, and that matters more than any unverified “premium” story. In the current EPREL snapshot, the market contains **60,989 listings from 777 manufacturers**, but only **537 models use R290** versus **13,935 using R32**. That puts R290 at **0.88%** of listings, while R32 holds **22.85%** — roughly **26 times** as many models.

That gap is the core finding. Buyers comparing propane and R32 are not choosing between two evenly matched segments; they are comparing a small, policy-favored refrigerant pool against a mature mainstream one. R290’s GWP is **0**, while R32 sits at **771** and carries a listed phase-out date of **2027-01-01** in the reference table. The market implication is straightforward: R290 may be the regulatory direction of travel, but breadth of choice is still on R32’s side.

The harder question is whether R290 commands a price or efficiency premium. The short answer from this dataset is: **not enough evidence to say**. The corpus does not include refrigerant-level list prices, and it does not provide refrigerant-specific SCOP averages. The only market-wide efficiency benchmark available is an overall **4.55 average SCOP**, which is useful context but not enough to rank R290 against R32 directly. Brand-level data adds a useful filter. Across all listings, **Bosch Thermotechnik** posts an average SCOP of **4.69** on **3,602 models**, and **Ariston** sits at **4.66** on **2,618 models**, both above the market average. Those are portfolio-wide numbers, not refrigerant-specific rankings, but they show where stronger efficiency profiles are concentrated.

For buyers and analysts, the conclusion is structural: R32 still dominates by count, R290 remains small, and any claim about a propane price premium should be treated cautiously unless it is backed by model-level data.
