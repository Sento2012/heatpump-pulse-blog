R290 is showing up more often in EPREL, but the data does **not** support the idea that propane has already taken the European catalogue. The latest snapshot contains **537 R290 listings** versus **13,935 R32 listings** across a **60,989-model** database, so R32 still outweighs R290 by a wide margin. Even if the small `R290A` and `R290a` variants are added, propane-coded entries rise only to **540**.

The more useful question is not whether R290 “won,” but how manufacturers are positioning it inside their catalogues. The listing base is highly concentrated: **Daikin Europe N.V.** holds **14,668 models** or **24.05%** of the full database, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575** and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207**. The top five manufacturers together account for **51.93%** of all listings, which means refrigerant strategy is being set by a small number of large players.

That concentration matters because the R290 presence is not limited to the biggest catalogues. In the top 15 R290 models by listed minimum power, Mitsubishi appears four times, while smaller distributors such as **Global Income Sp. z o.o.**, **Arc E-commerce se**, and **Comtrade Distribucija d.o.o.** also show up multiple times. The visible propane surface is therefore more fragmented than the overall brand leaderboard suggests.

The missing piece is price. The corpus behind this analysis contains no pricing dataset, so it cannot quantify whether R290 carries a premium, where that premium is largest, or which brands make it affordable. That absence is the key finding: we can measure refrigerant share and catalogue concentration, but not the economics of adoption.

For buyers and policy watchers, the practical takeaway is to treat refrigerant counts as a structural signal, not a proxy for accessibility.
