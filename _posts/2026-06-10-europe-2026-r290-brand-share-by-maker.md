R290 has crossed into the visible part of Europe’s heat-pump market, but it is still far from the dominant design choice. In the current EPREL snapshot, propane appears in **537 listings**, which is only **0.88%** of the **60,989-model** universe. By comparison, **R32** alone accounts for **13,935 listings** — roughly **26x** more than R290 — while legacy **R410A/R410a** still totals **1,945**. The signal is clear: the refrigerant transition is real, but it is still narrow and uneven.

That unevenness matters because the market is highly concentrated. The largest catalog owner, **Daikin Europe N.V.**, holds **14,668 models** or **24.05%** of all listings. The next largest portfolios — **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575**, **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207**, **Bosch Thermotechnik** at **3,602**, and **Ariston** at **2,618** — bring the top five to **51.93%** of the full market. In other words, the brands with enough scale to shift refrigerant demand are a small group, not the long tail.

The live data also shows where the transition has to happen: mainstream air-based products. EPREL is dominated by **air-to-water heat pumps** (**30,452 listings**) and **air-to-air** units (**21,065**), with **9,228** heat-pump water heaters. Ground-water and water-water systems are tiny segments by comparison. Since the snapshot does not expose refrigerant-by-type cross-tabs, it cannot prove exactly which segment carries the most R290, but the structure of the market makes clear that any real propane expansion must be absorbed by these high-volume categories.

Performance data does not show an obvious penalty for the bigger players either. The market-wide average SCOP is **4.55**, while several large manufacturers sit at or above that level, including **Bosch** at **4.69** and **Ariston** at **4.66**. That weakens the idea that lower-GWP refrigerants necessarily mean weaker seasonal efficiency.
