The clearest signal in EPREL’s 2026 noise data is not a single “quietest” model, but a strong category effect: **water-water units average 42.0 dB**, about **19.3 dB below the market average of 61.3 dB**, while **air-water sits at 59.8 dB** and **air-air at 64.1 dB**. That means the quietest paper specs are concentrated in small, niche categories rather than the mainstream residential pool.

The live EPREL snapshot behind this analysis covers **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers**. At the extreme low end, the top-listed model is **GESTION INTEGRAL DE ALMACENES, S.L. HTW-MKT2-V500**, declared at **1 dB** outdoor sound power. Just behind it, a large cluster of entries from **WAMAK, s.r.o.** also reports **1 dB**, and that brand occupies **11 of the top 15** quietest positions. That concentration matters: the quietest results are not evenly distributed across the biggest sellers, but clustered around a few manufacturers and product lines.

The market mix also skews the comparison. EPREL currently lists **30,452 air-water models** and **21,065 air-air models**, versus only **213 ground-water** and **31 water-water** units. So while the quietest category on paper is water-water, the volumes are too small to treat it as a benchmark for the broader market.

Noise and efficiency are not moving in opposite directions here. The market-average **SCOP is 4.55**, but several quiet WAMAK units score well above that, including **6.8**, **6.75**, and **6.67**. The quietest models in this dataset also tend to be large-capacity machines, with several listings in the **30–69 kW** range, far above the **9.3 kW** market average.

For readers comparing specs, the key point is structural: quiet operation is visible in EPREL, but it clusters by type, capacity, and brand rather than tracking the largest manufacturers.
