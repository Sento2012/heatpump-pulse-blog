R290 is not yet the dominant refrigerant across Europe’s declared EPREL heat-pump universe — but the market structure is moving in that direction, and the shift is highly concentrated. In the 2026 snapshot, Europe shows 60,989 listed heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers, yet only 537 models are explicitly tagged R290, compared with 13,935 tagged R32. That gap is the key signal: the transition to propane exists, but the visible inventory is still heavily weighted toward legacy refrigerants.

The bigger story is distribution, not just totals. Europe’s catalog is unusually concentrated at the top: Daikin Europe N.V. alone accounts for 14,668 models, while the top five manufacturers collectively hold 31,670 models, or 51.93% of all listings. That means any refrigerant transition led by a few large suppliers can reshape what installers see in tenders much faster than a fragmented long tail would suggest. The [full analysis](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-r290-share-by-brand-and-refrigerant) breaks down why that matters for procurement and product screening.

At the same time, the data has limits that are worth noting. The refrigerant counts in this EPREL snapshot sum to just 14,529 models, leaving a large share of the 60,989-model universe without a visible refrigerant code in the corpus slice. So while R290’s presence is real, the dataset does not support a blanket claim that propane has already overtaken all other refrigerants across Europe.

For buyers and engineers, the practical takeaway is simple: treat refrigerant transition as a catalog-filtering problem, not a settled market outcome. The searchable R290 pool is far smaller than the R32 pool, and the market impact will depend heavily on which of the largest brands are carrying new propane models.
