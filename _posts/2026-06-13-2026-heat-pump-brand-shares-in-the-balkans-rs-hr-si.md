The main conclusion is methodological: the EPREL snapshot does **not** contain country-level model counts, brand shares, refrigerant splits, or SCOP/noise averages for **Serbia, Croatia, or Slovenia**, so a true Balkan ranking cannot be built from this corpus alone. What *is* visible is the broader European structure those markets sit inside — and that structure is highly concentrated.

Across the full EPREL base, Househeating Pulse tracks **60,989 listed models** from **777 manufacturers**, with an average **SCOP of 4.55** and average outdoor noise of **61.3 dB**. The top five manufacturers already account for **51.93%** of all listings, led by **Daikin** at **14,668 models (24.05%)**, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric** at **5,575 (9.14%)** and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207 (8.54%)**. That concentration matters: any Southeast European country slice is likely to reflect supplier strategy more than a broad, even market base. See the underlying market context here: [Househeating Pulse Market Index](https://househeating-pulse.com/insights/market-index).

Refrigerants tell a similarly narrow story. Declared usage is dominated by **R32**, with **13,935 listings** or **84.91%** of declared refrigerant entries. **R290** appears only **537 times** (**3.27%**), and even after folding in spelling variants, propane-coded entries reach just **540** (**3.29%**). In other words, low-GWP natural refrigerants remain a small minority in the registry, despite regulatory pressure under EU F-gas rules.

Efficiency also does not line up neatly with brand size. **Bosch** and **Ariston** are the only top-five brands above the market’s **4.55 SCOP** average, at **4.69** and **4.66** respectively. By contrast, **Daikin** posts **4.44**, **Mitsubishi Electric** **4.51**, and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** **4.18**. That makes the largest brand the broadest, not the most efficient, by average listing profile.
