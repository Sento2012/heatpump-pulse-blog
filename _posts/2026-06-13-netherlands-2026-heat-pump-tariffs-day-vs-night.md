## Dutch heat-pump economics in 2026 are still driven more by tariffs than by subsidies

The Netherlands is a strong case for *timing-aware* electrification: household electricity sits at **€0.2558/kWh**, gas at **€0.1719/kWh**, and the resulting **1.49 electricity-to-gas ratio** is one of the most favourable in Europe for heat pumps. That matters more than the headline grant level, because the main active subsidy is only **€2,750**. In other words, Dutch operating economics are being shaped by tariff structure, not by large upfront support.

That relative pricing is why a heat pump can still compete well on running cost even without a generous grant regime. At a simple **SCOP 4** assumption, each thermal kWh costs about **€0.064/kWh-th** in electricity input, while gas remains structurally more expensive on a delivered-heat basis. The full analysis at [househeating-pulse.com](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/netherlands-2026-heat-pump-tariffs-day-vs-night) shows how far the Dutch market sits below the simple SCOP-4 break-even line of **4.0**: the current ratio is only **37.3%** of that threshold.

The key engineering point is load shifting. Even without separate Dutch day/night household tariff figures in the dataset, the economics are sensitive enough that a meaningful off-peak discount can change annual operating cost materially. A **€0.10/kWh** cheaper tariff on **50%** of consumption lowers blended electricity cost by **€0.05/kWh**, which translates to roughly **€0.0125/kWh-th** less heat cost at SCOP 4. A larger spread or a higher share of shifted load produces proportionally larger savings.

This helps explain why the Netherlands can remain attractive for heat pumps despite only moderate support: gas is expensive enough relative to power that smart controls, storage, and tariff-aware scheduling can matter as much as equipment choice. The market is not defined by cheap electricity; it is defined by a comparatively low power-to-gas ratio.
