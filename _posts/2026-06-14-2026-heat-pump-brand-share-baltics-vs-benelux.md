The sharpest finding is not a brand race but a market-structure gap: the EPREL extract does **not** actually contain the country-filtered manufacturer data needed to prove which brands lead the Baltics versus Benelux. What it does show is that the wider European catalogue is already highly concentrated, which makes regional dominance plausible even when the bloc-level ranking is missing. The full dataset contains **60,989** listed models, and [Daikin Europe N.V.](https://househeating-pulse.com/manufacturers/daikin-europe-n-v) alone represents **24.05%** of them with **14,668** entries and an average SCOP of **4.44**.

That concentration matters because the next tier is much smaller: Mitsubishi Electric is at **9.14%**, Johnson Controls Hitachi at **8.54%**, Bosch at **5.91%**, and Ariston at **4.29%**. Together, the top five hold **51.93%** of all listed models. For buyers and specifiers, that suggests regional dealer catalogues are likely to be shaped by a short list of manufacturers rather than a flat long tail.

The strongest bloc-level signal in the source is actually price structure. Five of the six countries studied sit below the rough **3.7** electricity-to-gas break-even marker. Belgium is the exception at **3.90**, while the Netherlands is far lower at **1.49**. The Baltics cluster more tightly: Lithuania **2.86**, Latvia **2.97**, Estonia **3.03**. In Benelux, the spread is wider because Dutch gas remains relatively cheap compared with electricity.

Refrigerants are harder to compare cleanly because EPREL declarations are inconsistent, but the regulatory direction is clear: the reference table spans **15** refrigerant codes and shows rapid phase-out pressure on legacy gases like **R410A** and **R32**. The current top-efficiency models also cluster tightly, with SCOP values from **6.92 to 7.00**, though the registry does not expose refrigerant for those entries.
