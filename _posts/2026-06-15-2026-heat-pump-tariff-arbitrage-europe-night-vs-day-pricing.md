The key finding is not that night tariffs automatically save money — it’s that **most households need a very specific combination of climate, price structure, and controllable load before tariff arbitrage becomes material**. In the 2026 data, the gap between markets is driven more by baseline economics than by any universal “cheap night electricity” story.

The coldest markets in the corpus are Norway at **5,039.96 HDD18**, Iceland at **5,028.63**, Estonia at **4,474.47**, Lithuania at **4,423.05**, Finland at **4,407.92**, and Latvia at **4,407.08**. Those are the places where shifted heat-pump runtime has the largest potential kWh volume. But the operating case still depends on local retail pricing: the dataset does **not** include a harmonized day/night tariff registry, so the analysis focuses on where arbitrage *would* matter rather than ranking exact spreads. The canonical article expands the logic here: https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-tariff-arbitrage-europe-night-vs-day-pricing

What can be measured is the broader electricity cost environment. Among the heat-pump-relevant countries highlighted, household electricity sits at **€0.3869/kWh in Germany**, **€0.2561/kWh in France**, **€0.2558/kWh in the Netherlands**, and **€0.2709/kWh in Poland**. Across the full dataset, Ireland is highest at **€0.4042/kWh** and Hungary lowest at **€0.1082/kWh**, a spread of **€0.2960/kWh**. That matters because a larger base tariff requires a bigger off-peak discount to generate the same euro savings.

The electricity-to-gas ratio is the second filter. Markets below the **3.7** threshold include Sweden (**1.3**), the Netherlands (**1.49**), France (**1.78**), Italy (**2.0**), and Germany (**3.16**). By contrast, the UK sits at **4.63** and Romania at **5.11**, where tariff arbitrage has to overcome a much weaker running-cost position.

Subsidies can improve payback, but they do not create day/night savings. The practical takeaway is simple: check the tariff sheet, then test it against local heating demand and control flexibility.
