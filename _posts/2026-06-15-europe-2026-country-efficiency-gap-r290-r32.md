The main finding is not a verified country-by-country winner for R290 versus R32. It is that the dataset is too lopsided to support a clean refrigerant-efficiency ranking: **R32 appears on 13,935 models**, while **R290 shows up on just 537**. That is a roughly **26:1** model-count gap, so any simple comparison would be dominated by product mix, not just refrigerant chemistry.

That matters because the broader market is still large and heterogeneous. The snapshot covers **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers**, with an overall average SCOP of **4.55**. Within the mix, **air-water systems** account for **30,452 models** at **4.54 SCOP**, while **ground-water** averages **4.77** and **water-water** reaches **6.15** on only **31 models**. In other words, refrigerant labels are sitting inside a market where system type already moves efficiency more than small chemistry differences often do.

The country economics add another layer. On the electricity-to-gas price ratio, **Romania leads at 5.11**, followed by the **United Kingdom at 4.63** and **Belgium at 3.9**. At the lower end, **Sweden is at 1.3**, the **Netherlands at 1.49**, and **Portugal at 1.73**. That spread means a 0.2 SCOP difference has very different value depending on where the system is installed.

The key analytical point is therefore structural: refrigerant choice, product type, climate, and tariff regime all interact. Without a country-by-country SCOP cross-tab for R290 and R32, the market can’t yet support claims about the widest or smallest national gap. But the evidence does show where that gap would matter most economically — and why the installed base is still far from a settled European standard.
