Austria, Croatia and Romania split cleanly on one metric that matters most for switching economics: the electricity-to-gas price ratio. Austria is lowest at **2.68**, Croatia follows at **3.05**, and Romania is far higher at **5.11**. Using a **3.7** break-even reference for a SCOP 4 heat pump, Austria and Croatia still sit below the line, while Romania is **1.41 points above it**. That is the sharpest signal in the comparison.

The tariff picture is only part of the story. Austria combines the most expensive household electricity in the group at **€0.3272/kWh** with the strongest subsidy position: **one active programme** and a maximum recorded support level of **€23,000**. Croatia is the low-cost outlier on absolute prices, at **€0.1658/kWh** for electricity and **€0.0543/kWh** for gas, but it has **no active subsidy recorded**. Romania sits between them on electricity at **€0.2893/kWh**, yet its gas price is still only **€0.0566/kWh**, which leaves the switching ratio structurally weak.

Climate and grid data reinforce the pattern. Austria is the coldest market of the three at **3309.19 HDD₁₈**, but it also has the cleanest grid at **89 gCO₂/kWh**. Croatia and Romania are warmer, at **2957.86** and **2734.85 HDD₁₈**, yet their grids are materially dirtier: **134 gCO₂/kWh** and **240 gCO₂/kWh** respectively. The result is a mature-market profile for Austria, a lower-cost but less-supported case for Croatia, and a harder economics case for Romania.

The EPREL catalogue context is broad — **60,989 models** from **777 manufacturers** across Europe — but the country comparison is less about catalogue depth than local viability.
