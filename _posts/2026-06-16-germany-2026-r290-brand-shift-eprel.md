Germany’s R290 segment is still small, but that is exactly why its brand ordering matters: when a niche slice moves, it often signals a portfolio push rather than a broad-market trend. Across the declared EPREL refrigerant universe, R290 accounts for **537 listings** versus **13,935 for R32**, so propane remains only a narrow part of the catalog. That makes any reshuffle in Germany worth watching closely.

The broader EPREL brand table shows how concentrated the market still is. **Daikin Europe N.V.** leads with **14,668 models** and **24.05%** share, far ahead of **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575 models** and **9.14%**, and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207 models** and **8.54%**. But the key question is not who leads the entire market — it is whether Germany’s R290 ranking is now following a different logic than the wider catalog.

One useful pattern from the full database: volume and efficiency do not move in lockstep. Daikin’s average SCOP is **4.44**, while Mitsubishi Electric posts **4.51** and **Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH** reaches **4.69** on just **3,602 models**. That gap suggests that a brand can expand listings aggressively without being the efficiency leader, and vice versa. For propane, that distinction matters because a brand can gain share through broader registration effort, not just better performance.

The available data does **not** expose Germany-only R290 winner/loser tables, pricing, or before/after comparisons, so the reshuffle cannot be quantified here. What the numbers do show is the structural backdrop: a low-share refrigerant segment, large differences in listing scale, and only modest SCOP dispersion across major brands.
