Portugal is not the bigger Iberian market, but it is the one with the cleaner operating-cost signal. The clearest figure in the dataset is the electricity-to-gas ratio: **1.73** in Portugal versus **2.79** in Spain, compared with a rough **3.7** break-even benchmark for a SCOP 4 unit. That makes Portugal materially closer to heat-pump-favorable economics even before any policy effects are layered in.

The market structure around that signal is still highly concentrated. Across the tracked European EPREL snapshot there are **60,989** listed models, and the top three manufacturers hold **41.73%** of all listings: **Daikin** at **24.05%**, **Mitsubishi Electric** at **9.14%**, and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **8.54%**. That concentration matters for smaller markets like Portugal because a limited number of registration decisions can move the mix quickly, even if we cannot directly prove a Portugal-only listing share from the supplied data. See the full country context on the [canonical analysis](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/2026-heat-pump-market-shift-portugal-vs-spain).

Refrigerants are the second structural clue. The market is still dominated by legacy gases: **R32** appears on **13,935** listings, while **R290** is only at **537**. Natural refrigerants are just **3.27%** of the total snapshot. If Portugal is accelerating faster than Spain, refrigerant choice is the most likely place to see it first, because the direction of travel in the catalog is already visible even without country-level splits.

Efficiency signals point in the same direction, though again only at Europe-wide level. Average SCOP across the snapshot is **4.55**, with **water-water** systems at **6.15** and **air-water** at **4.54**. Energy class is also stacked toward the top end, with **23,466** listings in **A+++**.

Portugal therefore looks like the stronger candidate for a faster mix shift, but the available corpus proves the economic setup more than the country-specific outcome. **Read the full analysis with live data on the canonical page**, including the EPREL context and the structural reasons Portugal may move ahead of Spain.
