The clearest signal in the 2026 EPREL snapshot is not which brand is largest, but how weakly brand scale predicts efficiency once you segment by output. Daikin dominates the registry with 14,668 listings, or 24.05% of the market, yet the best air-to-water SCOP values in the visible top band come from less familiar names such as Risch, Master Therm, ProCalor, Walter Bösch, Kermi and Hoval. That is the core reason this analysis focuses on size bands rather than brand averages.

The dataset is large enough to matter: 60,989 heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers, including 30,452 air-to-water units. Across the full registry, average SCOP is 4.55 and average declared power is 9.3 kW. But those averages hide the spread that matters for shortlist decisions. At 1.0 kW, visible models range from 3.15 to 4.77 SCOP; at 2.0 kW, from 2.75 to 4.97. By contrast, the higher-efficiency 35.0–77.0 kW cohort is much tighter, running from 6.42 to 6.97 SCOP.

That pattern is especially useful for buyers and installers comparing equipment in the same size class. NIBE’s 5.0 kW and 7.0 kW units reach 6.88 and 6.85 SCOP, while Master Therm posts 6.97 at 13.0 kW and 6.95 at 20.0 kW. Walter Bösch and Kermi both show 8.0 kW models at 6.90. These are not minor differences when you are narrowing a shortlist around capacity, noise and class.

The limitation is just as important: this EPREL corpus does not include purchase price, retail price or installation cost. So it cannot compute SCOP per euro or prove a price-efficiency correlation. What it can do is expose where high volume and high efficiency diverge, and where some low-volume brands outperform the names buyers often assume are safest.
