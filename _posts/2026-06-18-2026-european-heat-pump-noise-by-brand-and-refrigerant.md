The strongest signal in EPREL’s 2026 noise snapshot is not refrigerant choice — it is product type. Across 60,989 models from 777 manufacturers, the market average declared outdoor sound power is 61.3 dB(A), but the spread by family is much wider than any refrigerant pattern visible in this dataset. Water-water units average 42.0 dB(A), while air-air units average 64.1 dB(A), a 22.1 dB(A) gap that materially changes how a shortlist should be built.

That does not mean every low-noise declaration is equally credible. EPREL records declared sound power, not the acoustic experience at a property line or bedroom window, so installation geometry and operating mode still matter. Even so, the live registry contains very low declarations: the 20 quietest entries run from 1 dB(A) to 21 dB(A), and nine of the ten quietest model names are listed at 1 dB(A). The current leaderboard is here: [quietest models in EPREL](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-european-heat-pump-noise-by-brand-and-refrigerant).

Refrigerant is a weaker explanatory variable in this snapshot. Among the quietest 20, only 2 are labeled R290, 0 are R32, and 18 have no refrigerant recorded. That is not enough to support a robust “R290 is quieter” conclusion from noise data alone, even though R290 has a much smaller market footprint overall than R32 in the live EPREL universe.

For buyers, the practical reading is structural: prioritize product family, then model-level declarations, then siting. Brand scale also matters, but the brand tables in this analysis show share and efficiency, not a reliable brand-by-brand noise average.
