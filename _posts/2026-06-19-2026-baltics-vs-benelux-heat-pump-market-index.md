The biggest finding is a data gap: EPREL does **not** provide country-level averages for price, SCOP, refrigerant mix, or brand concentration for Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Belgium, and the Netherlands in this snapshot. So there is no defensible five-country ranking on cost or efficiency. What the data *does* support is a structural comparison: the Baltics sit in a much colder demand environment, while Benelux is milder and more subsidy-rich.

Heating degree days make that clear. Estonia records **4,474.47 HDD**, Latvia **4,407.08**, and Lithuania **4,423.05**, versus **2,934.26** in Belgium and **2,901.04** in the Netherlands. That is roughly a 1,500 HDD gap between the Baltic states and Benelux, which is large enough to influence system design, sizing, and the likely product mix. Electricity and gas prices also diverge: Lithuania is at **€0.1955/kWh** for electricity and **€0.0684/kWh** for gas, while Belgium is at **€0.3499/kWh** and **€0.0898/kWh**. The Netherlands is the gas outlier at **€0.1719/kWh**.

At EU level, the catalog is dominated by a few structural patterns. [Air-water heat pumps](https://househeating-pulse.com/heat-pumps?type=air-water) account for **30,452 models** or **49.93%** of the snapshot, with average SCOP around **4.54**. [R32 listings](https://househeating-pulse.com/heat-pumps?refrigerant=R32) make up **84.80%** of declared refrigerant entries, while natural refrigerants remain only **3.27%**. On the supplier side, Daikin alone holds **24.05%** of listed models, and the top three brands together reach **41.73%**.

The practical takeaway: any Baltics-versus-Benelux price story is more likely to reflect portfolio composition, climate load, and market concentration than a simple “cheaper means worse” rule.
