The key finding is not that quieter heat pumps are rare, but that quietness is concentrated in specific segments of the EU catalog rather than evenly spread across the market. In the current EPREL snapshot, Househeating Pulse tracks **60,989 models** from **777 manufacturers**, with a market-average declared outdoor sound power of **61.3 dB**. Against that baseline, the quietest listings fall to **1 dB**, and the quietest 15 are dominated by **air-water** equipment.

That concentration matters for model selection. Of the 15 quietest entries, **13 are air-water** and **2 are air-air**, even though air-water accounts for about **49.9%** of the full catalog and air-air for **34.5%**. In other words, air-water makes up **86.7%** of the quietest 15, which is materially above its market share. The quietest group is also heavily skewed toward the top heating label: **12 of 15 are APPP**, versus **38.5%** of the overall market.

Brand scale does not map cleanly to quietness. The largest registrant, **Daikin Europe N.V.**, has **14,668 models** and **24.05%** share, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575 models** and **9.14%**. But the quietest records in this snapshot are led by **WAMAK, s.r.o.**, which occupies **11 of the 15** quietest positions. That is a useful reminder that volume leaders are not automatically the noise leaders.

The one thing this dataset cannot answer is the price question. The supplied EPREL fields do **not** include list prices, so there is no defensible way here to compute a quiet-model premium, a price/noise correlation, or a “best value” ranking. The structural signal is still useful: if you are filtering for low noise in 2026, the quietest edge of the market is disproportionately found in air-water, APPP-labelled models, not across all product classes.
