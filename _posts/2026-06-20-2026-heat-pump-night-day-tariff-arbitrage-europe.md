The strongest signal in the latest tariff comparison is not who has the cheapest electricity overall, but where night operation actually changes the economics. Among the markets with usable day/night data, **France** shows the largest modeled spread at **7.18 c/kWh**, ahead of **Germany** at **6.97 c/kWh** and **Spain** at **4.81 c/kWh**. That gap is large enough to matter for heat-pump dispatch, especially when control strategy can move a meaningful share of load into off-peak hours.

Relative to each market’s day-rate anchor, the spread is about **28.0% in France** (7.18 c on 25.61 c), versus roughly **18.0% in Germany** (6.97 c on 38.69 c) and **18.0% in Spain** (4.81 c on 26.69 c). In absolute terms, the savings per shifted kilowatt-hour are straightforward: **€0.0718/kWh** in France, **€0.0697/kWh** in Germany, and **€0.0481/kWh** in Spain. For a household using **3,000–5,000 kWh/year**, that implies a full-shift annual difference of about **€215–€359** in France, **€209–€349** in Germany, and **€144–€241** in Spain.

The broader market context still matters. Average household electricity prices in the latest semester range from **10.82 c/kWh in Hungary** to **40.42 c/kWh in Ireland**, but the operational question here is narrower: whether a time-of-use tariff makes overnight heat-pump running materially cheaper than daytime operation. On that metric, the answer is yes in the three markets above, and especially in France.
