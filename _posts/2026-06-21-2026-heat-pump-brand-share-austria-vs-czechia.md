The sharpest takeaway is not that Austria and Czechia have clearly different EPREL market structures — it’s that the public extract does **not** support that claim yet. The country-level brand-share, refrigerant mix, and price-tier fields needed for a true Austria-vs-Czechia comparison are missing, so any confident statement about “more consolidated” or “more fragmented” local catalogues would be speculative.

What the data **does** show is a useful operating contrast. Czechia is the colder market, with 3,539.76 HDD18 versus 3,309.19 in Austria, and a mean January temperature of -2.18°C compared with -1.64°C. That is not a massive climate gap, but it is enough to move heating-load assumptions. The economics diverge more clearly: electricity is almost the same at €0.3217/kWh in Czechia and €0.3272/kWh in Austria, while gas is materially cheaper in Czechia at €0.0961/kWh versus €0.1221/kWh in Austria. Austria partly offsets that with much stronger support, including a headline subsidy cap of €23,000 under “Raus aus Öl,” compared with €4,900 for both NZÚ Light and NZÚ Standard in the Czech data.

At the EU-wide EPREL level, the market is still heavily concentrated. Daikin Europe N.V. leads with 14,668 models, or 24.05% of all listings, followed by Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. at 9.14% and Johnson Controls Hitachi at 8.54%. The top five manufacturers account for 51.93% of the catalogue, which is a reminder that local buyer choice often sits on top of a very uneven supply base.

The refrigerant picture is similarly skewed at the overall level: R32 dominates with 13,935 listings, while R290 appears in just 537. That matters because the regulatory and efficiency implications differ sharply between high-GWP legacy refrigerants and lower-GWP alternatives.
