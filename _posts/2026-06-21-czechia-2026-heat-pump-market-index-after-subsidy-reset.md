Czechia no longer fits the “subsidy outlier” label on grant size alone. The current maximum active household support is **€4,900**, far below **Austria (€23,000)**, **Germany (€21,000)** and **Poland (€31,000)**. Yet the market still looks structurally favourable to heat pumps because the operating-price signal remains strong: household electricity is **€0.3217/kWh** versus gas at **€0.0961/kWh**, a ratio of **3.35**. That is the core 2026 pattern — less cash distortion, more tariff-driven economics.

The country is also not an easy climate outlier. Czechia’s **HDD18 is 3,539.76**, higher than Germany and Austria, but below Poland. In other words, it is a meaningful heating market without being a Nordic extreme. Combined with a grid intensity of **449 gCO2/kWh**, the policy case is not powered by exceptionally clean electricity either.

For the product side, the European listing universe is still dominated by mainstream air-to-water systems. Across EPREL, there are **30,452 air-to-water models** versus **21,065 air-air**; ground-water and water-water remain niche at **213** and **31** models. Average SCOP for air-to-water units is **4.54**, which gives a practical baseline for the segment most relevant to Czech retrofit demand. The most efficient listed units reach about **6.92–7.0 SCOP**, but those are clearly the high end, not the median.

Brand concentration is also strong at EU level: **Daikin** leads with **14,668 models** and **24.05% share**, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric** at **9.14%** and **Hitachi** at **8.54%**. That concentration matters because it shapes what Czech buyers actually see in the catalog, even though the dataset does not expose Czechia-only brand shares.
