The main signal is not that low-GWP refrigerants have arrived at scale; it is that the EU’s heat-pump catalog is still concentrated in legacy chemistry. In the current EPREL snapshot, **R32 alone covers 13,935 listings, or 22.85% of the catalog**, while **R410A adds 1,896 listings (3.11%)** and **R290 reaches just 537 listings (0.88%)**. Natural refrigerants as a group still sit at **3.27%** of declared listings.

That matters because the market’s leakage-risk exposure tracks volume as much as chemistry. R32 sits in the middle on climate impact with **GWP 771**, but its sheer prevalence makes it the dominant stock risk; R410A is worse on GWP at **1,924** and remains material in declared volume. R290 is the opposite case: **GWP 0**, but only a small share of listings. The trade-off is not theoretical, either — R290 is **A3** (highly flammable), while R32 is **A2L** and R410A is **A1**. Lower GWP does not eliminate installation complexity.

Type data points to where the transition is most commercially relevant. **Air-water heat pumps dominate with 30,452 models**, compared with **21,065 air-air** and **9,228 hot-water** units. The efficiency frontier is narrower: **water-water averages SCOP 6.15** across only **31 models**, while **air-water averages 4.54** at industrial scale. That combination — large volume plus decent efficiency — is why the refrigerant transition will be decided first in air-water, not in niche segments.
