The EPREL 2026 dataset suggests a structural, not accidental, split: the quietest heat pumps are **not** the same units that lead the efficiency ranking. Across **60,989** listed models from **777** manufacturers, average outdoor noise is **61.3 dB** and average SCOP is **4.55** — but the top of each leaderboard diverges enough to matter for buyers optimizing for acoustics versus seasonal efficiency.

On the quiet end, the **15 lowest-noise models** average just **1.3 dB** outdoors, yet their average SCOP is **5.52** where SCOP is reported. The **15 highest-SCOP models**, by contrast, average **6.94 SCOP** and show **0.0 dB** outdoor noise in EPREL. That 0 dB figure should be treated cautiously: it likely reflects reporting conventions or missing acoustic entries rather than literal silence. Still, the dataset does not support a simple “quiet costs efficiency” rule; it shows that the two shortlists are populated by different products, with roughly **1.4 SCOP points** separating them on average. Type-level aggregation makes the pattern clearer. **Air-water** systems dominate the catalog at **30,452** listings, averaging **4.54 SCOP** and **59.8 dB**. **Ground-water** units are smaller at **213** models but improve slightly to **4.77 SCOP** and **58.8 dB**. **Water-water** systems are rare at **31** models, yet they stand out at **6.15 SCOP** and only **42.0 dB** — a combined gain of **1.61 SCOP points** and **17.8 dB** versus mainstream air-water units.

The practical takeaway is straightforward: shortlist by system type first, then compare individual models on both SCOP and declared sound power. Quietest leaderboard entries and best-efficiency entries are not interchangeable, and EPREL’s extreme acoustic values warrant a second look before they’re treated as real-world performance.
