## Portugal vs Greece vs Austria: the market signal is stronger on economics and climate than on catalog structure

The sharpest finding is not that one of these three markets clearly has the broadest EPREL shelf. It’s that the available data supports a **cross-country cost-and-climate split**, while the product-mix claims remain unproven. On the tariff side, Portugal is the most favorable of the three for heat-pump economics: electricity is **EUR 0.2434/kWh** versus gas at **EUR 0.1405/kWh**, an electricity-to-gas ratio of **1.73**. Greece is at **2.59** and Austria at **2.68**, so all three sit below the article’s rough **3.7** break-even threshold for a SCOP 4 unit, but Portugal has the widest margin.

Climate points in the opposite direction. Austria carries **3,309.19 heating degree days**, far above Greece at **1,152.59** and Portugal at **851.63**. That makes Austria the heaviest-heating market in the trio, while the southern markets face a more mixed heating/cooling load. Grid carbon also separates the profiles: Austria is lowest at **89 gCO₂/kWh**, compared with **153** in Portugal and **360** in Greece. In other words, Austria looks structurally colder but cleaner on electricity, while Portugal looks easiest on running-cost arithmetic.

The catalogue baseline is EU-wide, not country-specific: **60,989** heat-pump models from **777** manufacturers, with **A+++** alone accounting for **23,466** listings and **Daikin** leading the brand table at **14,668 models**. That context matters, but the corpus does not provide country-level EPREL counts, SCOP averages, refrigerant shares, or brand concentration for Portugal, Greece, or Austria. So claims about which market is more mature on model diversity or lower-GWP adoption cannot be validated from this dataset.

The most defensible conclusion is narrower: Austria looks strongest on heating need, grid carbon, and subsidy support, while Portugal offers the cleanest tariff case for heat pumps relative to gas.
