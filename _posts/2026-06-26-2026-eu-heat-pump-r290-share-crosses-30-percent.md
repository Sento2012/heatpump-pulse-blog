The key signal is not that R290 is “winning” everywhere, but that it has crossed a threshold that changes how the EU heat-pump catalog should be read: **30.30% of declared space-heating listings in the live EPREL slice are now propane-based**, up to **537 models out of 1,772**. That is enough to move R290 from fringe status to a material part of the competitive set, even though **R32 still dominates with 13,935 listings** in the broader declared refrigerant pool.

That split matters because the pressure on refrigerants is now coming from both compliance and product planning. In the reference table, **R290** is a natural refrigerant with **GWP 0** and no listed F-gas phase-out date, while **R32** carries **GWP 771** and a **2027-01-01** phase-out date. Legacy HFCs such as **R410A** and **R134a** are even more exposed. In the catalog, the R410A family still sums to **1,955 listings** when variant spellings are combined, but that is a declining base rather than a growth signal.

For buyers and installers, the practical question is not just refrigerant type, but what it implies for the rest of the product stack. The live market average SCOP is **4.55**, and the strongest volume segment — **air-water heat pumps** — averages **4.54 SCOP** across **30,452 models**. That suggests propane is entering the most commercially important efficiency battleground, not a niche corner of the market.

Pricing conclusions are harder to make from the data alone: the corpus does not include model-level prices for all R290 units. But the structural backdrop still favors low-GWP platforms, especially in markets where electricity remains expensive relative to gas, such as **Germany (3.16)**, **Poland (3.71)** and **Belgium (3.90)** on the electricity-to-gas ratio.
