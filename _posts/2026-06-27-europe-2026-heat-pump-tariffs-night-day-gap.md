The main finding is simple: **time-of-use pricing matters most where heating demand and daytime electricity costs are already high**. In the 2026 tariff data, the biggest operational leverage comes from shifting compressor load into off-peak hours, not from changing the heat pump itself.

Among the four-country day-night dataset, **Germany** has the largest absolute spread at **€0.1244/kWh** in the latest half-year, with tariff levels of **€0.3869/kWh by day** and **€0.2625/kWh at night**. **Poland** is close behind at **€0.1170/kWh**, while **Sweden** combines a **€0.0940/kWh** gap with the clearest colder-climate profile. **France** is the weakest case for arbitrage, with only **€0.0776/kWh** separating day and night prices.

That translates directly into avoided electricity cost per shifted kilowatt-hour: roughly **12.44 cents in Germany**, **11.70 cents in Poland**, **9.40 cents in Sweden**, and **7.76 cents in France**. For homes with thermal mass, buffer tanks, or underfloor systems, those differences can be material. For low-inertia buildings, the same tariff spread may not be large enough to justify much control complexity.

The broader context supports that view. In the 32-country comparison, cold markets such as **Sweden, Finland, Norway, Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania** combine long heating seasons with meaningful electricity costs, making tariff structure a real part of system economics. The same data also shows that the gap is not static: since 2018 H1, **Poland’s day-night spread widened by €0.0666/kWh**, while **Germany’s narrowed by €0.0364/kWh**.

A second filter is the electricity-to-gas ratio. Most reported markets still sit below the rough **SCOP 4** hurdle, but **the UK (4.63)** and **Romania (5.11)** are above it, which raises the value of load shifting where it exists.
