The main signal in the smaller EU heat-pump markets is not uniform growth; it is a clear split between countries where running costs still work and countries where they do not. In 2026, **22 markets sit below the roughly 3.7 electricity-to-gas price ratio** where a **SCOP 4** heat pump can outperform gas on operating cost, while **Poland is effectively on the line at 3.71** and **Romania is well above it at 5.11**. That threshold is the cleanest way to separate markets that still have economic room from those where adoption now depends much more on policy support.

The best small-market pricing environments are concentrated in a few very different geographies: **Sweden at 1.3**, **Portugal at 1.73**, **Bulgaria at 2.09**, **Slovenia at 2.44**, and **Greece at 2.59**. None of these are the EU’s largest markets, but they are among the clearest cases where household electricity-versus-gas economics still favor heat pumps. A useful way to track the country-level detail is through the [full country comparison dashboard](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/2026-eu-heat-pump-market-index-outside-big-15).

Subsidies are also uneven, and in some cases they are doing most of the work. **Poland’s maximum support reaches €31,000**, far above **Austria’s €23,000** and **Ireland’s €6,500**. Poland’s scheme can cover up to **100% of cost** in the top tier and runs until **2029-12-31**, which helps explain why a market sitting near the price-ratio threshold can still remain active.

On the product side, the broader market pool points to mature hardware rather than a race to extremes: across **60,989 listed models**, the average **SCOP is 4.55**, average capacity is **9.3 kW**, and average outdoor noise is **61.3 dB**. The mass market remains **air-water**, which is less efficient than niche water-water systems but overwhelmingly more available.
