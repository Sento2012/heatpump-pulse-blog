Austria is the outlier in this three-market set, but not because the registry proves it has the widest catalog. The sharper signal is economic: Austria combines the highest household electricity price in the group at **€0.3272/kWh** with the largest recorded subsidy ceiling, **€23,000**. Hungary sits at **€0.1082/kWh** with no active subsidy recorded, while Slovakia is in between on power price at **€0.1853/kWh** and has a **€3,400** maximum subsidy. That combination makes operating-cost math and grant capture materially more important in Austria than in its neighbors.

Climate does not materially separate the three markets. Austria is tagged at **3,309.19 heating degree days**, versus **3,060.58** in Hungary and **3,035.92** in Slovakia — a spread of only **273.27 HDD** from the warmest to the coolest. In other words, commercial positioning, energy tariffs, and support schemes are likely to matter more than weather in shaping buying decisions.

What this comparison does *not* support is a clean ranking on model choice, brand concentration, refrigerant mix, inverter share, or country-level SCOP. Those country splits are not present in the supplied corpus, so any claim that one market has a deeper EPREL catalog than the others would be speculative. The Europe-wide baseline, however, shows a market still dominated by conventional refrigerants: natural refrigerants account for just **3.27%** of indexed listings, while declared **R290** entries number **537** compared with **13,935** for **R32** and **1,896** for **R410A**.

For efficiency context, the broader EPREL universe puts average SCOP at **4.55**, with product-type spreads much larger than the Austria-Hungary-Slovakia climate spread.
