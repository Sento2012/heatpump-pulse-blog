France currently has the stronger 2026 heat-pump setup than Italy on the variables that matter most for adoption: running-cost economics, subsidy depth, and carbon intensity. The core tariff signal is straightforward: France’s electricity-to-gas ratio is **1.78**, versus **2.00** in Italy, so a heat pump is better aligned with French household energy prices before any subsidy is even applied. France also has a much higher support ceiling, at **€11,000** against Italy’s **€5,000**.

That combination matters more than headline market size. Italy remains a major southern market, but its warmer climate means lower annual heating demand, while France’s heating load is materially larger at **2,759.65 HDD18** compared with **1,536.47 HDD18** in Italy. On emissions, France’s grid is also much cleaner: **56 gCO₂/kWh** versus **226 gCO₂/kWh** in Italy. For buyers comparing economics and policy together, France is the more favorable environment.

The break-even test for a SCOP 4 heat pump reinforces the same conclusion. Both countries are below the **4.00** electricity-to-gas threshold, so both can support heat pumps on operating cost alone. But France is closer to the better end of the curve, and its implied delivered-heat cost is lower than Italy’s under the same simplified assumptions. The difference is not dramatic, but it is directional.

Brand structure does not overturn that picture. Across the European EPREL pool, the top 5 manufacturers account for **51.93%** of listed models, with **Daikin** alone at **24.05%**. That suggests buyers in both countries are still shopping from a broad market rather than facing a country-specific product lock-in.
