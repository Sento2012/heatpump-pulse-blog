The core finding is not a ranking of the “best” night tariffs — the dataset does not contain enough dual-rate price detail to make that claim. What it *does* show is that load shifting only matters when the base electricity price is high enough for an off-peak discount to have real weight. In 2026, that puts **Ireland at €0.4042/kWh**, **Germany at €0.3869/kWh**, and **Belgium at €0.3499/kWh** at the top end of the opportunity range, while **Hungary (€0.1082/kWh)** and **Bulgaria (€0.1355/kWh)** sit much lower. See the full country context in the [canonical analysis](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-night-vs-day-tariffs-in-europe-10-markets).

That distinction matters for heat-pump owners because a fixed discount in cents per kWh scales very differently across markets. A 5 c/kWh night tariff gap is structurally more valuable in a system starting from €0.39/kWh than one starting near €0.11/kWh. The article therefore treats off-peak shifting as a tariff-arbitrage problem first, and a heat-pump problem second.

The broader Europe-wide picture also supports a split strategy. Electricity-to-gas ratios remain compressed in some markets — **Sweden at 1.3**, **the Netherlands at 1.49**, **France at 1.78** — while they are much less favourable elsewhere, including **Romania at 5.11** and **the UK at 4.63**. That means automation, thermal storage, and smart controls are not universally justified by the same logic; the economics depend on the local tariff stack before any control hardware is added.

Historical averages reinforce the trend. Since 2018 H1, household electricity prices rose by **€0.0899/kWh in Italy**, **€0.0882/kWh in Germany**, and **€0.0813/kWh in France**, while **Spain** increased by only **€0.0286/kWh**. Base prices have moved up enough that even moderate time-of-use spreads can still matter in some markets.
