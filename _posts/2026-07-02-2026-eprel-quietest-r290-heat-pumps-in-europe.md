The quietest declared R290 heat pumps in EPREL come from a very small corner of the market: 537 propane listings out of 60,989 total heat-pump entries, or just **0.88%** by count. That matters because the headline ranking is not sampling the European market broadly; it is isolating a niche where acoustic performance appears unusually strong, but coverage is thin.

The most striking pattern is type concentration. **All 10 models in the quietest-R290 shortlist are air-air units**. None of the quietest entries are air-water or ground-source products, which suggests that low declared outdoor sound power is currently being concentrated in smaller, room-scale designs rather than in the larger heating categories. The range is also extreme: the top model is listed at **2 dB(A)**, while rank 10 sits at **59 dB(A)**. That 57 dB spread is large enough to raise questions about how declarations are being made, so buyers should verify the individual model documentation before treating any single number as final.

The cluster below the top two is still notably quiet relative to the category. After the 2 dB(A) and 20 dB(A) outliers, the remaining models in the top 10 mostly sit between **50 and 59 dB(A)**. On a market basis, that is below the broader **air-air average of 64.1 dB(A)**, even though EPREL’s overall heat-pump market averages **61.3 dB(A)** outdoors.

Brand concentration is equally sharp. **SC Trade & Services GmbH appears three times** in the top 10, while **Schuss Home Electronic GmbH appears twice**. The largest EPREL manufacturers — including Daikin, Mitsubishi Electric and Hitachi — do not show up in this particular shortlist.
