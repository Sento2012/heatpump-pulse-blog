R290 has moved from edge case to measurable segment in EPREL, but the key signal is still the gap to the incumbent refrigerants. The database now contains **537 R290 listings out of 60,989 total models** — just **0.88%** overall — yet that is large enough to track as a distinct product pool rather than a handful of experimental entries. By comparison, **R32** still dominates with **13,935 listings** (**22.85%**), while all other declared refrigerants combined sit at **3.36%**. The shift is real, but it is still early.

The important context is that EPREL is a registration database, not a sales ledger. So these counts show what manufacturers are choosing to list, not what has actually been installed in homes. Even so, the rise of propane is meaningful because the natural-refrigerant segment now represents **3.27%** of all listed models, which means R290 is no longer standing alone inside a tiny low-GWP niche.

The market structure behind that shift is also concentrated. EPREL includes **777 manufacturers**, but the top five — led by **Daikin Europe N.V.** with **14,668 models** — account for **51.93%** of all listings. That concentration matters because refrigerant adoption often follows portfolio strategy: once large vendors commit to a code path, the catalog changes fast. The current corpus does not provide a brand-only R290 ranking, so the question of which manufacturers are driving propane specifically remains open.

From a policy and procurement angle, R290’s appeal is straightforward: **GWP 0** and no listed EU phase-out date, versus **R32** at **GWP 771** with a phase-out date of **2027-01-01**, and **R410A** at **GWP 1,924** with a **2025-01-01** date. That does not automatically make R290 the best-performing option, but it does make it the cleaner compliance choice.
