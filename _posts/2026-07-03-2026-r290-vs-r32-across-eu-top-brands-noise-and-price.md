R290 is still a minority signal in EPREL, not the market default. In the 2026 extract, R32 appears on 13,935 listings versus 537 for R290 — roughly 25.95 R32 records for every one R290 record, with R290 making up just 3.71% of the clean two-code set. That gap matters more than the headline “R290 transition” narrative: the data supports a strategic shift, but not a wholesale replacement.

The brand picture is equally asymmetric. EPREL volume is concentrated in a few manufacturers, led by [Daikin Europe N.V.](https://househeating-pulse.com/manufacturers/daikin-europe-n-v) with 14,668 models and 24.05% of the listed universe. Mitsubishi Electric Europe follows at 5,575 models (9.14%), while Bosch Thermotechnik sits at 3,602 (5.91%). Those shares define where any refrigerant migration would have the largest visibility, but the registry extract here does **not** join refrigerant choice to brand at the needed level. In other words: we can see scale, and we can see refrigerant totals, but not yet a clean brand-by-brand R290 share.

On performance, the top manufacturers are not interchangeable. Among the largest eight brands, average SCOP ranges from 4.18 for Johnson Controls Hitachi to 4.69 for Bosch Thermotechnik, with Ariston at 4.66 and Vaillant at 4.54. That spread is large enough to influence shortlist decisions even before considering refrigerant, noise, or climate fit.

Noise and price are less conclusive in the registry. EPREL’s quietest entries include some extreme outliers — several WAMAK models are declared at 1 dB with SCOP values up to 6.80 — but these are not representative of the mainstream brand set. Price is not recorded in the source at all, so there is no quantified premium for R290 or for quieter designs.

For buyers and installers, the practical takeaway is simple: R290 is strategically important, but the 2026 catalog is still R32-heavy.
