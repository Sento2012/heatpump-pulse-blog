## Night-shifting still works — but mostly where heating loads are large enough to matter

The key result is not that time-shifting has disappeared; it’s that the economics are now highly climate-dependent. In 2026, running a heat pump harder at night only looks attractive where there is both a real off-peak discount and enough heating demand for the savings to scale. In mild bands, the kWh you can move are simply too few to move the needle.

The data points to a clear north–south split. Colder markets in the comparison set cluster around **4,415 heating degree days (HDD18)** at the median, versus **1,153 HDD18** in warmer markets — a gap of **3,262 degree days**. That difference is the structural reason night-shifting remains a real operating strategy in northern Europe and fades into marginal optimisation further south. Sweden, for example, shows **4,242 HDD18** and a mean January temperature of **-3.08°C**, while Germany is milder at **3,308 HDD18** and **-0.73°C** in January.

Electricity price levels also shape the payoff. Germany’s blended household tariff is **EUR 0.3869/kWh**, compared with **EUR 0.2711/kWh** in Sweden and **EUR 0.1922/kWh** in Norway. That means a displaced daytime kWh is worth more in some high-price markets, but the colder climate can also increase the night-time COP penalty. The break-even point is therefore not a single European rule; it depends on local tariffs, weather, and how much thermal storage the building can use.

The practical takeaway is simple:  
- **Cold climates:** tariff-aware controls, weather compensation, and modest pre-heating can still be worthwhile.  
- **Average climates:** optimisation may work, but only if the tariff spread is real and the building can absorb it.  
- **Warm climates:** the annual load is often too small for night-heavy scheduling to produce meaningful savings.
