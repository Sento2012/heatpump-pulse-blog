The main 2026 signal is not a neat Europe-wide ranking of night-versus-day discounts; it is that **most heat-pump economics are now decided by the electricity-to-gas ratio before tariff timing even matters**. Out of 26 markets with both inputs, **23 sit below the ~3.7 threshold** where a SCOP 4 heat pump can undercut gas on running cost. Only **Belgium (3.9), the UK (4.63) and Romania (5.11)** are clearly above it, while **Poland is almost exactly on the line at 3.71**.

That makes dedicated off-peak tariffs most relevant in borderline cases, not across the board. In Poland, even a moderate night discount could be enough to tip operating costs in favor of a heat pump. Belgium is the next closest market where time-of-use pricing still has meaningful leverage. But in the UK and Romania, tariff shaping alone is less likely to erase the structural gap.

The baseline price level also matters. Late-2025 household electricity prices were **0.3869 €/kWh in Germany**, compared with **0.2561 €/kWh in France** and **0.2558 €/kWh in the Netherlands**. Yet absolute electricity price is only part of the story: France and the Netherlands still look favorable because gas is also relatively expensive there, at **0.1436 €/kWh** and **0.1719 €/kWh** respectively. Germany, despite high power prices, still lands at **3.16** on the electricity-to-gas ratio because gas is cheaper at **0.1223 €/kWh**.

That is the key structural point: off-peak pricing can improve heat-pump economics, but the bigger determinant is whether the market starts from a favorable retail ratio at all. Countries like **Sweden (1.3)**, **the Netherlands (1.49)**, **Portugal (1.73)** and **France (1.78)** do not need large night discounts to make the case work.
