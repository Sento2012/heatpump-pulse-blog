Norway comes out as the strongest structural market of the three, not because it has the biggest subsidy, but because its operating economics are aligned with electrified heating: low household power prices, very low grid carbon, and the highest heating load. Ireland is the least favourable on the same screen, with Denmark sitting in the middle.

The pricing spread is the first signal. Household electricity is €0.1922/kWh in Norway, €0.3312/kWh in Denmark, and €0.4042/kWh in Ireland. That leaves Ireland €0.2120/kWh above Norway, a gap large enough to reshape payback math before installation quality is even considered. Gas pricing narrows the comparison to Denmark and Ireland: €0.1257/kWh in Denmark versus €0.1300/kWh in Ireland. On the tariff-ratio test, Denmark lands at 2.63 and Ireland at 3.11, both below the 3.7 break-even threshold referenced in the analysis, but Ireland is materially closer to it.

Climate and grid data push the same way. Norway records 5039.96 heating degree days, compared with 3447.07 in Denmark and 3010.73 in Ireland. At the same time, its grid intensity is just 18.0 gCO₂/kWh, versus 142.0 in Denmark and 287.0 in Ireland. In other words, Norway combines the highest heating demand with the cleanest electricity, which is a strong fit for heat pumps even without a recorded gas benchmark.

The product-side backdrop is also useful. Across the current EPREL snapshot, Househeating Pulse counts 60,989 listed heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers, with an average SCOP of 4.55. That catalog is dominated by air-water units, and market power is concentrated: Daikin alone holds 24.05% of listed models, while the top five manufacturers account for 51.93%.

For buyers, installers, and analysts, the key point is that national economics and product availability are not the same thing. The country comparison shows why some markets convert more easily than others, while the EPREL data shows what the broader equipment universe can actually deliver.
