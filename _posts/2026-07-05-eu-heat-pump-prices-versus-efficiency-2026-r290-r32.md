The main 2026 signal from EPREL is not that “efficient units cost more.” It is that **price, refrigerant choice, and declared efficiency are only loosely coupled**, and the gap between the market’s volume centre and its performance frontier is still wide.

EPREL now contains **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers**, but the average unit sits at only **SCOP 4.55**. That is materially below the best propane entries, while the average declared capacity is **9.3 kW** and average outdoor sound power is **61.3 dB**. In other words, the catalogue is large, but the mass of listings remains clustered around mid-range performance rather than the top end.

The segment split matters. **Air-to-water** dominates with **30,452 models**, followed by **air-to-air** at **21,065**; by contrast, **ground-water** accounts for just **213** models and **water-water** for **31**. Yet the most efficient average class is not the biggest one: **water-water averages SCOP 6.15**, ahead of **ground-water at 4.77** and **air-water at 4.54**. The strongest efficiency class is also the smallest, which makes marketing claims easy to overread if you only look at common residential listings.

Refrigerant counts show a similar structure. **R32** is still the volume leader with **13,935 models**, while **R290** is much smaller at **537** — roughly a **26:1** gap. But the declared environmental profile is inverted: **R290 has GWP 0**, while **R32 is listed at GWP 771** and a **2027-01-01** phase-out date. That does not prove a price-performance winner by itself, but it does show why refrigerant narratives and catalogue reality often diverge. The economics depend heavily on country tariffs too: **Sweden (1.30)**, **Netherlands (1.49)**, and **France (1.78)** are far below the **3.71** electricity-to-gas ratio where a SCOP 4 unit starts to look competitive on running cost.
