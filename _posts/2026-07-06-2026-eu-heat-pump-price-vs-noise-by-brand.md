The key finding is not that one big brand wins on quietness — it is that the quietest EPREL entries are often coming from specialist or smaller manufacturers, while the volume leaders dominate model count rather than the low-noise tail. In the 2026 snapshot, the registry covers **60,989 models from 777 manufacturers**, with a whole-market average declared outdoor sound power of **61.3 dB**. Against that baseline, the main buying question is whether quiet operation is concentrated in premium lines or can be found without a clear price penalty; the full EPREL dataset does not expose price fields for a definitive answer, but the noise structure is still informative.

Market concentration is high. **Daikin Europe N.V.** leads with **14,668 models** and **24.05%** share, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575 models** and **9.14%**, and **Hitachi/JCI Europe** at **5,207 models** and **8.54%**. Yet the 25 lowest-noise listings are not led by those brands. Several **WAMAK** models appear at **1 dB** declared sound power, and the quiet list also includes a **Panasonic** test entry at **5 dB**. That is a strong signal that declared quietness is not exclusive to the largest suppliers.

Type selection matters as much as brand selection. **Air-water** heat pumps average **59.8 dB**, while **air-air** units average **64.1 dB**; **ground-water** units sit at **58.8 dB**. The gap between air-water and air-air is **4.3 dB**, which is material in practical siting terms. Air-water also matters commercially: it is by far the largest segment at **30,452 models**, making it the most relevant place to look for a quiet-but-mainstream shortlist.

Refrigerant patterns are less decisive than many buyers assume. The market still leans heavily toward **R32** with **13,935 models**, while **R290** appears in only **537**. Quiet models do include some R290 entries, but the extract is too incomplete to claim a refrigerant-driven quietness premium.
