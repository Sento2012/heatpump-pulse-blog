Austria is already a mature heat-pump market, but the 2026 EPREL snapshot suggests the next decision point is not adoption volume — it is whether propane-based R290 models are delivering enough measurable upside to justify a higher purchase price.

The baseline is sizeable: the live catalog contains 60,989 models from 777 manufacturers, with an average SCOP of 4.55 and average declared outdoor noise of 61.3 dB. That sits in a country with 3,309 heating degree days and a mean January temperature of -1.64°C, so the market is being judged in genuinely heating-heavy conditions, not in a mild climate where almost any unit looks good. The dominant product class remains air-to-water, at 30,452 models, which is the segment most relevant to Austrian boiler replacement.

R290 is still niche, but not negligible. EPREL lists 537 models explicitly tagged R290, or about 0.88% of the full universe; even if you include minor code variants, propane only reaches 540 models. By contrast, R32 sits at 13,935 models and R410A at 1,896. That gap shows R290 is still far from mainstream, yet large enough to test whether it is winning on performance rather than just regulatory positioning.

The regulatory and economic context explains why this matters. The refrigerant table gives R290 a GWP of 0, while R32 is listed at 771. At the same time, Austria’s electricity-to-gas ratio is 2.68, which is below the approximate 3.7 break-even line for a SCOP-4 heat pump. In other words, operating economics already support efficient heat pumps; the unanswered question is whether the front-end premium for R290 is still buying better efficiency, lower noise, or only a higher sticker price.

Austria’s subsidy framework is also material, with “Raus aus Öl” reaching €16,000 for a single-family home and up to €23,000 for low-income households. That can soften premium pricing, but it does not automatically erase it.
