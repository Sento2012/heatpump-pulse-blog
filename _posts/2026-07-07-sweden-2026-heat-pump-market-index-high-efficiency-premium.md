Sweden is an outlier not because heat pumps are simply “expensive” there, but because the operating economics are unusually supportive while the market still looks technically demanding. The country’s electricity-to-gas price ratio is **1.3**, far below the roughly **3.7** break-even level highlighted in the analysis, and its grid is exceptionally clean at **14 gCO₂/kWh**. Add **4,242** heating degree days and Sweden becomes a strong test case for whether premium performance can be sustained without depending on subsidies.

That combination matters. Household electricity is **€0.2711/kWh** and gas is **€0.2092/kWh**, so Sweden’s running-cost spread is narrow in absolute terms but highly favourable for heat pumps relative to gas. Compared with several large European markets, the signal is clear: Germany is at **3.16**, Belgium at **3.9**, the UK at **4.63**, and Romania at **5.11** on the same ratio metric. Sweden is not just below the threshold; it is on the most heat-pump-friendly side of the European comparison.

The broader market context also helps explain why Sweden matters. Across **60,989** EPREL-listed models from **777** manufacturers, the European average SCOP is **4.55**, and **80.7%** of models are A+ or better. That means Sweden’s market premium, if present, is likely to be about product mix and efficiency positioning rather than category scarcity alone.
