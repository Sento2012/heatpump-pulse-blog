The main signal is that heat-pump capacity scales far faster than efficiency. Across EPREL’s top-scoring samples, the jump from 2–6 kW to 12+ kW multiplies average declared capacity by about 9.0x, but average SCOP rises only from 5.43 to 6.66. That is a useful reminder for buyers: bigger units do deliver more output, yet the efficiency dividend is modest.

Househeating Pulse’s latest market snapshot puts the broader registry at 60,989 listed models from 777 manufacturers, with an all-type average SCOP of 4.55 and average declared capacity of 9.3 kW. Against that backdrop, the three size bands studied here — 2–6 kW, 6–12 kW and 12+ kW — look less like a single continuum and more like distinct market segments with different type mixes. Air-to-water systems dominate the catalog with 30,452 listings, or 49.93% of the total, while water-water remains tiny at just 31 listings, but carries the highest average SCOP at 6.15.

That segmentation matters. The 2–6 kW sample averages 5.10 kW and SCOP 5.43, the 6–12 kW sample averages 9.18 kW and SCOP 6.21, and the 12+ kW sample averages 45.98 kW and SCOP 6.66. In other words, the step from small to mid-size gains 4.08 kW of capacity for less than one SCOP point, while the step into 12+ kW adds 36.8 kW for only 0.45 SCOP more. The efficiency curve is flattening, not accelerating.

Brand concentration is also visible in the registry. Daikin Europe N.V. alone accounts for 14,668 listed models, or 24.05% of the market, while Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. has 5,575 and Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH 3,602. That scale helps explain why mid-size air-to-water models dominate the mainstream set, even when niche water-water systems set the upper end of SCOP performance.
