The clearest signal from the latest EPREL snapshot is that **R290 is still an adoption layer, not a market reset**. Plain-labeled propane entries account for only **537 models**, or about **3.3%** of declared refrigerant records, versus **13,935 R32** entries and **1,896 R410A** entries. That gap matters: the European heat-pump catalog is still structurally dominated by legacy refrigerants, even as low-GWP options become more strategically important.

At the brand level, the catalog remains concentrated. **Daikin Europe N.V.** leads the top-10 readout with **14,668 models**, equal to **24.05%** of the total **60,989** entries covered. The next four brands are far behind: **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575**, **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207**, **Bosch Thermotechnik** at **3,602**, and **Ariston** at **2,618**. That spread shows that scale is still the main lever in EPREL visibility, even before refrigerant composition is considered. Efficiency does not perfectly track catalog size. Among the top five brands, average SCOP ranges from **4.18** for Johnson Controls Hitachi to **4.69** for Bosch, with **Ariston at 4.66**, **Mitsubishi at 4.51**, and **Daikin at 4.44**. In other words, the largest catalog is not the most efficient fleet in this slice, which leaves room for smaller players to compete on performance even if they remain smaller by count.

The implication is straightforward: **R290 is becoming strategically relevant before it becomes numerically dominant**. The dataset does not yet expose brand-by-brand propane shares, so it cannot prove which manufacturer is converting the transition into the biggest internal R290 footprint. But it does show the market context: incumbents still control the catalog, and the refrigerant mix is still overwhelmingly R32-based.
