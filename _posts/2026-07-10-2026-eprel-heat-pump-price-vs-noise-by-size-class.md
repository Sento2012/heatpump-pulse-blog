# EPREL’s 2026 heat-pump noise ladder looks flatter at the low end and steeper at the high end

The clearest signal in the 2026 EPREL sample is not a price premium — the corpus contains **no price field at all** — but an acoustic split by capacity. In the **2–6 kW** band, the plausible quiet cluster sits around **32–36 dB(A)**, while the **12+ kW** threshold records visible in the extract are typically around **60–69 dB(A)**. That is a structural gap large enough to matter for siting, even before installation variables enter the picture.

The low-capacity side also shows why registry data needs filtering. The quietest surfaced record is a Panasonic entry at **5 dB(A)**, and the corpus also contains **0 dB(A)** declarations in the small-unit and high-capacity slices. Those values should be treated as registration anomalies, not real-world acoustic benchmarks. If you ignore the implausible outliers, the 2–6 kW class is anchored by STIEBEL ELTRON models at **32 dB(A)**, with nearby Bosch and Nofenta records in the **35–36 dB(A)** range.

The middle band, **6–12 kW**, is where the spread becomes most useful for buyers. One clean comparison in the extract shows **6.0 kW** models ranging from Mitsubishi Electric at **55 dB(A)** to a BDR Thermea record at **69 dB(A)** — a **14 dB** difference at the same nominal capacity. That is a material gap for semi-detached and tighter urban installs, and it suggests that brand, product architecture and fan design matter as much as rated output.

The larger **12+ kW** segment trends louder in the more credible entries, with multiple manufacturers clustering around **60–68 dB(A)**. The data cannot tell us whether quieter models command a higher euro price, but it does show where acoustic selection pressure increases with size.
