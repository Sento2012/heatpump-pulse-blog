The key finding is not that night tariffs no longer matter; it’s that the 2026 corpus does **not** support a clean Europe-wide ranking of day-vs-night heat-pump electricity spreads. What it does show is a very wide retail-price baseline, and that alone changes the economics of smart scheduling.

Across the 32-country comparison, household electricity ranges from **€0.1082/kWh in Hungary** to **€0.4042/kWh in Ireland**. Germany sits at **€0.3869/kWh**, while France is **€0.2561/kWh** and Spain **€0.2669/kWh**. In other words, a heat pump in Ireland is starting from a tariff level that is nearly **3.7x** Hungary’s before any off-peak discount is applied. That is why the structure of time-of-use pricing matters so much: a small percentage change is worth much more in absolute euros when the baseline rate is high.

The catch is methodological. The supplied 2026 data do not include separate day and night household tariffs by country, so it is impossible to calculate which markets have the largest gap, the smallest gap, or how many sit below a **10%** spread. The best available evidence is therefore indirect: use the market-wide electricity level as a proxy for where optimization potential is likely to be highest, then validate with local tariff data. The broader context is available on the [32-country comparison dashboard](https://househeating-pulse.com/insights/country-comparison), which is the most useful cross-market reference point in the current dataset.

Historical series for Germany, France, Spain, Italy and the Netherlands reinforce the same point: all-in electricity prices have moved materially since **2018-H1**. Germany is up **€0.0882/kWh**, Italy **€0.0899/kWh**, and Spain the least at **€0.0286/kWh**. The Netherlands also stands out for volatility, dropping to **€0.0338/kWh** in **2022-H1** before rebounding sharply.
