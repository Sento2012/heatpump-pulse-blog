The key finding is not a country ranking, but a data gap: EPREL does not expose compressor control type, so the 2026 registry cannot directly quantify inverter share by country or brand. That means the usual “which market leads?” question is still unanswered from the public corpus. What the data *does* show is the scale and structure of the market behind that missing metric: **60,989 heat-pump models** from **777 manufacturers**, with an average **SCOP of 4.55** and a catalog dominated by **air-water (30,452)** and **air-air (21,065)** products.

That matters because those two segments are where part-load modulation is most commercially relevant. In other words, inverter adoption would matter most in the very categories that make up the bulk of Europe’s listed products. The same snapshot also shows a refrigerant transition underway: **13,935 R32 models** versus **537 R290 models**, with natural refrigerants still only **3.27%** of listings.

Even without inverter fields, the market remains highly concentrated. **Daikin Europe N.V.** accounts for **14,668 models** and **24.05%** of EPREL listings, well ahead of **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575** and **9.14%**, and **Hitachi** at **5,207** and **8.54%**. That concentration means any future inverter-share analysis will be shaped by a few very large portfolios rather than a long tail of niche brands.

For buyers and installers, the more actionable signal today is tariff context. The electricity-to-gas ratio is **1.30 in Sweden**, **1.49 in the Netherlands**, and **1.78 in France**, versus **5.11 in Romania** and **3.90 in Belgium**. Those spreads explain why inverter-heavy systems would likely deliver the strongest economic benefit in some markets and much less in others.
