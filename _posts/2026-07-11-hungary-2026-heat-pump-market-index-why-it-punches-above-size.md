Hungary’s 2026 heat-pump signal is stronger than its market size suggests. The key metric is not volume but operating economics: the country sits at **3.23 on the electricity-to-gas ratio**, which is **below the ~3.7 break-even line** and better than several closer peers, including **Czechia at 3.35** and **Romania at 5.11**. That puts Hungary in a tariff environment where heat pumps can compete on running cost before subsidies enter the picture.

The underlying price structure explains why. Hungary combines **€0.1082/kWh electricity** with **€0.0335/kWh gas**. Electricity is cheap enough relative to gas that the ratio stays heat-pump-friendly even without policy support. For comparison, **Czechia’s electricity price is €0.3217/kWh**, **Poland’s is €0.2709/kWh**, and **Slovakia’s is €0.1853/kWh**. On gas, Hungary is also at the low end of the regional set, so the market’s advantage is not a single-price anomaly but a relative-price effect. The full country comparison is here: [Hungary’s market profile and peer ranking](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/hungary-2026-heat-pump-market-index-why-it-punches-above-size).

Other structural indicators are directionally supportive. Hungary’s grid intensity is **192 gCO₂/kWh**, cleaner than **Poland (661)** and **Czechia (449)**, though not as low as **Slovakia (102)**. Climate demand is also material, at **3,060.58 heating degree days**, so this is not a mild-climate edge case.

The policy layer is notably weaker. Hungary shows **no active heat-pump subsidy** in the current register, while nearby markets such as **Slovakia, Czechia, and Poland** do have active schemes. That means the Hungarian case is driven mainly by tariffs and system economics, not by grant pull.
