The main takeaway from the 2026 dataset is that **system design dominates brand choice**: the biggest efficiency spread is not between manufacturers, but between heat-pump classes and operating context. EPREL’s hydronic listings show **water-water** units leading on average with a **6.15 SCOP**, versus **4.77** for **ground-water** and **4.54** for **air-water**. But that top-performing category is tiny: only **31 water-water models** are listed, compared with **30,452 air-water models**. Scale and efficiency do not line up neatly.

That mismatch is why emitter and climate questions matter so much. The underlying analysis on [househeating-pulse.com](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-europe-heat-pump-efficiency-by-emitter-and-climate-zone) shows that the registry data here does **not** provide a radiator-vs-underfloor SCOP split, so the article focuses on the next best structural signals: temperature regime, tariff ratio, and product type. Those signals are strong enough to change the economics of the whole project.

Climate spread across Europe is wide enough to matter materially. In the country comparison, **Norway** reaches **5,039.96 heating degree days**, while **Malta** is only **492.27 HDD** — roughly a **10x** difference in heating demand indicator. At the same time, the electricity-to-gas price ratio ranges from **1.3 in Sweden** to **5.11 in Romania**, which directly shifts the break-even logic for a SCOP 4 system. In Sweden, the grid is also very clean at **14 gCO2/kWh**, making low-temperature electric heating especially attractive.

For buyers and installers, the implication is straightforward: start with emitter temperature and climate fit, then test tariff ratio and subsidy support, and only then compare product brochures. The full analysis connects those variables across Europe and shows where low-flow-temperature systems create the largest gains.
