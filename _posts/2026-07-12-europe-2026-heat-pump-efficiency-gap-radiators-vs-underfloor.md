The biggest takeaway from the EPREL snapshot is not a single headline efficiency number, but how strongly system design separates outcomes. Across the current European market, heat pumps with heating SCOP data average **4.55**, yet the air-to-water segment alone spans from **2.08** at the bottom of the leaderboard to **7.0** at the top — a **4.92-point** spread. That range is large enough to overwhelm many assumed gains from brand choice or marginal controls tweaks.

The original analysis also tests the claim that **underfloor heating can deliver about 18% better efficiency than radiators**, but the available dataset does **not** include the emitter-level split needed to verify that number. The direction is plausible — lower flow temperatures generally favor heat-pump operation — but the measured Europe-wide percentage gap should not be treated as confirmed without emitter-specific EPREL data. The full discussion is here: [https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-heat-pump-efficiency-gap-radiators-vs-underfloor](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/europe-2026-heat-pump-efficiency-gap-radiators-vs-underfloor)

The market structure helps explain why this matters. Out of **60,989** listed models in the broader snapshot, **30,452** are air-to-water units, making that category roughly half the catalog. By comparison, ground-water systems are a tiny niche, and water-water models are even smaller. In other words, the performance problem is concentrated in the segment most buyers actually face.

Type-level averages also show meaningful dispersion: air-water models average **4.54** SCOP, ground-water **4.77**, and water-water **6.15** — although the last figure is based on only **31** models. That is useful context, but it still does not replace emitter-level evidence.

On the economics side, the article notes that a SCOP-4 system beats gas on fuel cost when the electricity-to-gas price ratio is below **4.0**. Using that rule of thumb, **24 countries** clear the threshold; the UK (**4.63**) and Romania (**5.11**) do not. For retrofit planning, that means emitter choice, system temperature, and local energy prices should be evaluated together, not separately.
