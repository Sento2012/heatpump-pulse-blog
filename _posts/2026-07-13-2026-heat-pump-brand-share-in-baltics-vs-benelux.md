The sharpest finding is not that one bloc is “ahead”; it is that the supplied EPREL snapshot does **not** contain the country-filtered model counts needed to prove Baltics-versus-Benelux brand share. There is no bloc-level cross-tab for Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Belgium, the Netherlands or Luxembourg, so a numeric winner by region cannot be derived from the corpus. The full analysis on [Househeating Pulse](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-brand-share-in-baltics-vs-benelux) explains the gap and what can still be inferred.

What the dataset does support is a European baseline. At EU level, Daikin Europe N.V. leads with 14,668 listed models and a 24.05% share, far ahead of Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. on 5,575 models (9.14%). That is a 14.91 percentage-point gap, or 9,093 listings, between first and second place. The top three manufacturers together account for 41.73% of all listed models in the corpus, which is a useful reminder that EPREL breadth is already concentrated before any regional split is applied.

The same pattern shows up in product mix and refrigerants, but again only at EU-wide level. Air-to-water systems dominate the catalog with 30,452 listings, while air-air reaches 21,065 and heat-pump water heaters 9,228. On refrigerants, declared usage is led by R32 at 13,935 entries, with R290 still much smaller at 537. That matters because the regulatory direction is clear even if bloc-specific shares are missing: R290 is natural refrigerant territory, while R32 carries a higher-GWP profile and a tighter policy runway.

Tariff economics are one area where the two blocs can be compared directly. Using a rough breakeven threshold of 3.7 for a SCOP 4 heat pump, all three Baltic markets sit below it, while Benelux is split by Belgium’s 3.9 ratio. The Netherlands is especially favorable at 1.49.
