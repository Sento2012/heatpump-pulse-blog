The most important finding is not that R290 has already displaced R32 in Europe’s EPREL register — it hasn’t. In the latest snapshot, **R32 still appears in 13,935 models**, while **R290 is listed in 537**. That leaves R32 ahead by **22.0 percentage points** of the full model universe, with shares of **22.85%** versus **0.88%**. The real story is that the refrigerant transition is visible, but it is still early and uneven. For the full breakdown, see the canonical analysis at https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/2026-r290-share-vs-r32-in-europe.

The dataset behind this signal is broad: **60,989 heat-pump models from 777 manufacturers**. That makes the EPREL snapshot useful for reading registration behavior, not just marketing claims. It also shows how concentrated the market is. The top five manufacturers — **Daikin Europe N.V. (14,668 models)**, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V. (5,575)**, **Johnson Controls Hitachi (5,207)**, **Bosch Thermotechnik (3,602)** and **Ariston (2,618)** — together hold **51.93%** of all listed models. In other words, any meaningful refrigerant shift will likely be driven first by a small set of large portfolios.

The structural pattern matters too. **Air-water units make up 30,452 models**, or **49.93%** of the catalog, followed by **air-air at 21,065** and **hot-water heat pumps at 9,228**. The dataset does not provide a clean refrigerant-by-type table, so the strongest R290 segment cannot be proven numerically here. What it does show is that the mainstream product base is still dominated by established refrigerant coding, while R290 remains a much smaller declared slice.

There is also a compliance angle: the refrigerant reference identifies **R290 as propane with GWP 0**, while **R32 carries a GWP of 771** and a **2027-01-01 phase-out date** in the supplied table. That makes the direction of travel clear, even if the crossover is not yet complete.
