R290 is still a small slice of Europe’s heat-pump catalog, but the pace of adoption is clearly uneven: **537 of 60,989 EPREL-listed models** use R290, or **0.88%** of the universe, while natural refrigerants overall reach **3.27%**. That gap suggests a supplier-side shift is happening faster than broad market diffusion.

The concentration problem is visible in the core catalog. **R32 alone accounts for 13,935 models**, versus just **1,896 R410A** listings and a tiny R290 base. In other words, propane is not yet a system-wide standard; it is still a niche that a few manufacturers are pushing harder than the aggregate market implies.

Brand structure matters because the market is already concentrated before refrigerant choice is even considered. The top portfolios belong to **Daikin Europe N.V.** with **14,668 models**, **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** with **5,575**, **Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH** with **3,602**, and **Ariston SpA** with **2,618**. When the largest players change refrigerant strategy, the effect can be large even if country-level adoption remains fragmented. Country conditions help explain why propane adoption is not uniform. Electricity prices range from **EUR 0.1082/kWh in Hungary** to **EUR 0.4042/kWh in Ireland**, gas from **EUR 0.0335/kWh in Hungary** to **EUR 0.2092/kWh in Sweden**, and subsidies reach **EUR 31,000 in Poland** and **EUR 21,000 in Germany**. Those differences create very different heat-pump economics across Europe.

The practical takeaway: **R290’s growth is being led by portfolio decisions at a few brands, not by a synchronized European demand shift**.
