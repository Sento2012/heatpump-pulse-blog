R290 has crossed the threshold from pilot-code to visible market signal, but it has not displaced the incumbent. In the 16 July 2026 EPREL snapshot, propane appears on **537 models**, equal to **0.88%** of the **60,989-model** market — enough to show up across Europe, not enough to reshape catalog structure. By contrast, **R32** still sits at **13,935 models** and **22.85%** of the full market, making it the reference refrigerant by a wide margin.

That gap is the core story. R290 now represents most of the catalog’s natural-refrigerant presence, while total natural refrigerants together account for **3.27%** of listed models. In other words, the propane cohort is no longer marginal, but the market is still organized around conventional refrigerants. The canonical breakdown is here: https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/r290-gains-share-in-europe-2026-brand-signal

The brand angle is more subtle. EPREL shows heavy catalog concentration at the top — **Daikin Europe N.V.** leads with **14,668 models** (**24.05%**), followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575** and **Johnson Controls Hitachi** at **5,207**. Those manufacturers are large enough that refrigerant choice matters at system scale, but the dataset does **not** expose brand-level R290 counts. So the evidence supports a broad adoption trend, not a quantified claim that the biggest OEMs are already deeply retooled around propane.

Performance signals are also incomplete. The market-wide average SCOP is **4.55**, and the average declared power is **9.3 kW**, but the snapshot does not include an R290-only SCOP profile. That means the data can confirm visibility, not superiority.

Economics are more supportive. Using the article’s break-even framework, **23 markets** sit below the rough **3.7 electricity-to-gas ratio** threshold for a SCOP 4 heat pump. That makes low-GWP positioning easier to sell in much of Europe, even before subsidy effects are considered.
