Bulgaria’s EPREL footprint looks broad at first glance, but the catalog is still highly concentrated at the top. Out of **60,989 listed models**, the **top 10 brands account for 61.88%** of entries, and the **top three alone make up 41.73%**. That means the country’s visible heat-pump offer is shaped more by a few large manufacturers than by a long tail of niche suppliers.

The largest registry presence belongs to **Daikin Europe N.V.**, with **14,668 models** and **24.05% share**. **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** follows at **5,575 models** (**9.14%**), while **Johnson Controls Hitachi** is close behind at **5,207 models** (**8.54%**). If you compare breadth with declared performance, the spread is modest: among the top brands, average SCOP ranges from **4.18** for Hitachi to **4.69** for Bosch. That 0.51-point gap suggests catalog size varies more than declared seasonal efficiency.

The refrigerant picture is more concentrated still. **R32** dominates with **13,935 declared entries**, or **84.65%** of the refrigerant totals tracked here. **R410A** adds another **11.51%**, while **R290** remains a small but visible slice at **3.26%**. In practice, Bulgaria’s listed market is still overwhelmingly tied to higher-GWP refrigerants, even though R290 carries a GWP of 0 in the reference table. By system type, **air-water** units are the center of gravity with **30,452 models**, followed by **air-air** at **21,065**. The most efficient category on average is **water-water**, with **SCOP 6.15**, but it is tiny at just **31 models**.

For buyers and installers, the tariff signal matters too: household electricity is **€0.1355/kWh** versus gas at **€0.0648/kWh**, a ratio of **2.09**. That is below the roughly **3.7** break-even reference, which supports heat-pump economics on running costs.
