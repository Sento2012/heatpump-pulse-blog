The key result is simple: **overnight heat-pump operation still creates a real cost advantage in only part of Europe, and the margin is thinning in several large markets**. On the current Eurostat comparison, **22 countries sit below the 3.7 electricity-to-gas ratio** that roughly marks break-even for a **SCOP 4** system, while **Poland is effectively on the line at 3.71** and **four markets are above it**. That means the old “shift consumption to night rates and the savings will always follow” claim no longer holds uniformly.

The strongest tariff environments are still concentrated in the north-west and south-west of the sample. **Sweden leads at 1.3**, followed by **the Netherlands at 1.49**, **Portugal at 1.73**, and **France at 1.78**. At those levels, the electricity-gas spread leaves ample room for a heat pump to outperform gas on running cost, even before any off-peak discount is applied. By contrast, **Belgium (3.9), the UK (4.63), and Romania (5.11)** are already at or above the threshold, so time-shifting alone cannot make the tariff case look strong.

The difference between the best and worst markets is large: **3.81 ratio points** separate **Sweden’s 1.3** from **Romania’s 5.11**. That spread matters because absolute electricity price is not the whole story. **Ireland has the highest household electricity tariff in the dataset at €0.4042/kWh**, yet its ratio is still **3.11** because gas is also expensive. That is why ratio-based screening is more useful than looking at power prices in isolation.
