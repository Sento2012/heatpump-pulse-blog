The clearest signal in the 2026 EPREL snapshot is that **the most efficient cold-climate options are not confined to one architecture**. Water-water leads on average with **SCOP 6.15 across 31 models**, but the broader market is overwhelmingly air-water, with **30,452 listings** and an average **SCOP of 4.54**. For buyers in Europe’s coldest regions, that gap matters—but so does the fact that the top end is still heavily air-water: **11 of the top 15 SCOP models are air-water**.

That tension is the core of the analysis at [Househeating Pulse](https://househeating-pulse.com/guides/2026-heat-pump-coldest-european-regions-by-efficiency): niche technologies can post the strongest averages, yet the market depth and product availability remain concentrated elsewhere.

At the model level, the leaderboard is led by **Risch Kälte- und Klimatechnik OH I 4esr TWW W/W** at **SCOP 7.0**, followed by multiple **Waterkotte** units at **6.97**. The size spread is wide too: declared minimum power in the top 15 runs from **5.0 kW** to **46.0 kW**, which suggests that cold-climate performance is not tied to a single capacity band. All **15** top models carry **A+++ heating class**, so efficiency at the top is no longer the differentiator; the practical question becomes which product class fits the building and installer context.

Climate and pricing structure reinforce that point. The coldest countries in the dataset include **Norway (5,039.96 HDD)**, **Iceland (5,028.63 HDD)** and **Liechtenstein (5,023.68 HDD)**. Where gas-price ratios are available, several remain below the rough **3.7 break-even threshold** for a SCOP 4 heat pump—for example **Sweden at 1.3**, **Lithuania at 2.86**, and **Estonia at 3.03**.
