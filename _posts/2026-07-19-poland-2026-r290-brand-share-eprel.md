Poland is not yet a R290 market, but EPREL shows the refrigerant mix moving in that direction. Across the current European catalogue, R290 accounts for **537 models** versus **13,935 R32 models**; that still leaves propane at a small share, yet it is now large enough to track as a structural trend rather than a niche exception. In the same database, natural refrigerants represent just **3.27%** of all listed heat-pump models, so Poland’s signal is emerging inside a still R32-heavy baseline.

That matters because Poland combines strong heating demand with expensive electricity. The country profile used in the analysis shows **3,706 heating degree days** and an average January temperature of **-3.97°C**, alongside power pricing at **€0.2709/kWh**. In that context, small efficiency differences and refrigerant choice can move operating economics, especially for buyers comparing models over a long service life.

The brand side of the market is just as concentrated. The top five manufacturers hold **52.0%** of all listed models: **Daikin Europe N.V.** leads with **14,668** models, followed by **Mitsubishi Electric Europe B.V.** at **5,575**, **Hitachi** at **5,207**, **Bosch Thermotechnik GmbH** at **3,602**, and **Ariston SpA** at **2,618**. Efficiency is not aligned perfectly with scale: Bosch posts the highest average SCOP among that group at **4.69**, with Ariston close behind at **4.66**, while the all-market average sits at **4.55**.

The key point is not that R290 has overtaken R32 in Poland — it hasn’t — but that it has crossed the threshold where its presence is measurable and strategically relevant. For installers, buyers, and competitors, that changes the question from “will propane matter?” to “which brands are building the strongest position around it?”
