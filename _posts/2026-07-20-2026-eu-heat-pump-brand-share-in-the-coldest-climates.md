The main finding is structural: the coldest European heat-pump markets are not defined by climate alone, but by the interaction of heating demand, grid carbon intensity, and pricing. Norway and Iceland both exceed 5,000 HDD18, while Estonia, Finland, Lithuania, Latvia, and Sweden cluster between roughly 4,200 and 4,500 HDD18. Yet their carbon footprints diverge sharply, from 11 gCO₂/kWh in Iceland and 14 gCO₂/kWh in Sweden to 460 gCO₂/kWh in Estonia, which changes the emissions case for the same installed unit.

Economics are less hostile than many assume. In the cold-climate countries where gas prices are recorded, Sweden sits at 1.30 on the electricity-to-gas ratio, Denmark at 2.63, Lithuania at 2.86, and Estonia at 3.03 — all below the approximate 3.7 break-even line for a SCOP 4 heat pump. Austria and Liechtenstein also remain below that threshold at 2.68 and 2.37, respectively. That makes the north and the Alps more varied than a simple “cold equals uneconomic” narrative suggests. On the manufacturer side, EPREL still shows a heavily concentrated European market. Daikin Europe N.V. leads with 14,668 listed models and 24.05% share, followed by Mitsubishi Electric Europe at 9.14%, Johnson Controls Hitachi at 8.54%, and Bosch Thermotechnik at 5.91%. The top five manufacturers together account for 51.93% of all listed models, which is a meaningful signal in harsh-climate segments where breadth of certified variants matters.

Average SCOPs among major brands are also tightly grouped near the mid-4s, with Bosch at 4.69, Ariston at 4.66, and Mitsubishi Electric at 4.51. The corpus does not expose country-level brand shares or refrigerant mix by market, so the key takeaway is not a local ranking, but a platform-level one: cold-climate supply is concentrated, and the strongest portfolios combine scale with solid efficiency.
