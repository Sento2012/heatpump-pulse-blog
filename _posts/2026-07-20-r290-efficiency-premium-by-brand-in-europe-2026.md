The 2026 EPREL snapshot does **not** support a simple “R290 is more efficient than R32” claim. What it does show is a much bigger structural gap: **13,935 declared R32 models versus only 537 R290 models**. In other words, propane is still a very small part of the registered market, so any efficiency narrative is being built on a narrow sample.

That matters because refrigerant choice is only one layer of the performance story. Across the full dataset, **air-water heat pumps account for 30,452 models with an average SCOP of 4.54**, while **ground-water** units average **4.77** and **water-water** units reach **6.15**. The market is therefore shaped heavily by product type, and a raw refrigerant comparison can easily mix chemistry effects with portfolio composition.

Brand data reinforces that point. Among the largest manufacturers, **LG Electronics Deutschland GmbH** leads on average SCOP at **4.93** across **405 models**, followed by **STIEBEL ELTRON** at **4.84**. By contrast, **Daikin Europe N.V.** has the largest footprint with **14,668 models** and a **4.44** average SCOP, while **Hitachi’s European entity** sits at **4.18**. That spread is wide enough to matter more than many refrigerant headlines suggest. The full analysis is here: [R290 heat pumps in 2026: which brands are paying the efficiency premium?](https://househeating-pulse.com/articles/r290-efficiency-premium-by-brand-in-europe-2026)

The key limitation is methodological: the available data does **not** provide a clean refrigerant-by-SCOP split for all R290 versus all R32 models, nor a same-type comparison inside air-water units. So the evidence supports a cautious conclusion: **R290 is gaining visibility, but the efficiency premium appears to depend at least as much on brand and model line as on refrigerant alone**.
